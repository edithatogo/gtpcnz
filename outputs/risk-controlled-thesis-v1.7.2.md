# Risk-controlled thesis v1.7.2

## The revised claim
New Zealand's current primary care reform pathway is meaningful. It includes capitation reweighting, a primary care access target, the National Primary Care Dataset, urgent and after-hours care, digital access, Primary Health Organisation accountability work and separate appropriation structures.

The question is therefore not whether reform exists. It does.

The question is whether the current pathway changes the supply game enough.

## The proposed architecture
The preferred architecture is:

1. **Capitation for continuity and population responsibility.**
2. **An uncapped scheduled fee-for-service stream for eligible primary medical activity.**
3. **Place-based accountability to prevent cherry-picking and protect hard-to-reach populations.**
4. **Equity protections, including co-payment protections and Māori/Pacific review.**
5. **Scope-enabled supply, allowing appropriately credentialed providers to generate eligible activity within legal scope and clinical governance.**
6. **Data, audit and item-rule controls to prevent low-value activity and gaming.**
7. **Ambulance, urgent care and post-discharge pathways as upstream access infrastructure, not only hospital feeders.**

## The wording discipline
Use:

> uncapped at the global activity-envelope level, controlled at the item, scope, audit, co-payment, data and accountability level.

Avoid:

> uncapped funding

unless it is immediately qualified.

## What this is not
- Not a proposal to abolish capitation.
- Not a proposal to abolish Primary Health Organisations.
- Not a proposal to copy Medicare wholesale.
- Not a claim that Accident Compensation Corporation injury funding can simply be transferred to illness-based primary care.
- Not a calibrated prediction of hospital savings.
- Not a party-political claim.

## What would weaken the thesis
The thesis would be weakened if the current reform pathway demonstrably expands safe upstream supply, reduces closed books and waiting times, protects rural in-person care, improves access for Māori, Pacific and deprived populations, and reduces avoidable emergency department/ambulance/hospital flow without an activity-sensitive primary medical payment stream.

## What would strengthen the thesis
The thesis would be strengthened if stakeholder validation, OIA material or early National Primary Care Dataset evidence shows that reweighted capitation and access targets improve allocation/visibility but do not materially improve marginal appointment supply or upstream capacity.
