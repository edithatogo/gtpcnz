# What would change my mind? v1.7.2

This table is intended to make the project falsifiable rather than rhetorical.

| Claim | What would weaken it | Evidence needed |
|---|---|---|
| Dominant capitation can constrain marginal supply | Reweighted capitation plus current access targets materially increase appointment supply without new activity-sensitive payment | Appointment access, open-book status, encounter volume, co-payment and workforce data before/after reform |
| Unmet primary care need channels hospital growth | Access constraints do not predict emergency department, ambulance or admission flow after adjusting for need, rurality and deprivation | Linked primary care, ambulance, emergency department and inpatient data |
| ACC activity payments stabilise upstream supply | ACC payment changes have no material relationship with provider viability, activity or local access | ACC claims/contracts, practice revenue mix and provider activity data |
| PHO/payment intermediation creates friction | PHO pass-through is transparent, prompt and equitable, and new entrants face low administrative barriers | PHO financial/pass-through data, entry timelines, provider interviews and OIA material |
| Uncapped scheduled fee-for-service improves access | Activity growth is mainly low-value repetition rather than clinically useful access | Claims pattern analysis, clinical audit, re-presentation and patient outcome data |
| Place-based accountability prevents cherry-picking | High-need, rural, Māori, Pacific, disabled and complex patients remain under-served despite place accountability | Equity-stratified access, continuity and outcome data |
| Scope-enabled supply is safe and useful | Expanded provider eligibility fragments care or worsens safety/re-presentation | Provider-type outcomes, audit, adverse events and continuity measures |
| The current reform pathway is insufficient | Current reforms deliver access, equity, rural, urgent-care and hospital-pressure improvements without additional architecture change | Prospective post-2026 reform evaluation |
