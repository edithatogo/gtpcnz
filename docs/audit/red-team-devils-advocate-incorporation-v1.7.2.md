# Red-team and devil's advocate incorporation report v1.7.2

## Purpose
This release folds the v1.7.1 red-team and devil's advocate findings back into the launch-facing thesis, first-six Substack posts, decision-maker summary, model card, claim boundaries and Conductor system.

## Summary of changes
The project now treats the counterarguments as part of the argument, rather than as a separate appendix. The public position is deliberately narrower:

> Current reforms may be necessary but insufficient. The central question is whether they change the supply game enough. A scheduled, rules-based, audited fee-for-service component for eligible primary medical activity should be considered as one part of a hybrid architecture, not as a standalone replacement for capitation, place-based commissioning or equity-focused care.

## Incorporated red-team findings

| ID | Insight | Incorporation |
|---|---|---|
| RT01 | Overclaiming predictive effect | Every launch-facing model mention now states that outputs are scaffold/model-generated indices, not observed outcomes or calibrated predictions. |
| RT02 | Uncapped misread as uncontrolled | Every launch-facing statement of uncapping is paired with scheduled prices, item rules, scope, audit, clinical governance, co-payment protections and place accountability. |

| RT04 | RACMA distraction | RACMA remains excluded from active outreach; the pathway is public Substack, stakeholder validation, OIA/data requests and optional later engagement if RACMA approaches. |
| RT05 | Too many assumptions | The 70 parameters are framed as a tiered scaffold with a small core load-bearing set, not 70 equally important claims. |
| RT06 | PHO critique too broad | PHO critique is narrowed to function versus intermediation, pass-through opacity, non-capitated funding and entry/transaction cost. |
| RT07 | Current reform straw man | Current reform is now the explicit comparator; the claim is “necessary but possibly insufficient,” not “nothing is happening.” |
| RT08 | Unverified email intelligence | Email-trail material is labelled as stakeholder intelligence or OIA target unless independently sourced. |
| DA01 | Capitation reweighting plus targets may be enough | Posts and summaries now define what evidence would change the argument, including a successful current reform pathway. |
| DA02 | Place-based commissioning may be more important than benefits | The proposed architecture now treats place-based accountability as core, not optional. |
| DA03 | Rural access may require public/salaried/infrastructure levers | Decision-maker materials now state that scheduled FFS is not a full rural workforce policy and may need infrastructure, capital, housing and salaried models. |
| DA04 | Co-payments can worsen equity | Co-payment protections are a gating requirement and a core parameter, not a downstream add-on. |
| DA05 | Scope expansion may fragment care | Scope-enabled supply is conditioned on shared records, clinical governance, accountability and continuity. |
| DA06 | Hospital growth has multiple causes | The thesis is framed as upstream access being one plausible driver, not the sole cause of hospital growth. |
| DA07 | ACC transferability is limited | ACC is framed only as a rules-based treatment-payment analogy, not a model to copy wholesale. |


## Launch implication
Use the first six posts only for initial launch. The back half should remain held until feedback, equity review and stakeholder validation have been considered.

## Model implication
The parameterised model remains a scaffold. The model can help prioritise assumptions and validation work. It must not be used to claim exact savings, emergency department reductions or workforce effects.

## Engagement implication
RACMA is no longer an active path. Stakeholder validation, OIA requests, equity review and public critique are the next active mechanisms.
