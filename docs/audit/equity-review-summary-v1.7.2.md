# Māori, Pacific, and Equity Review Summary
**Version:** v1.7.2
**Date:** 2026-05-12

## Executive Summary
This document summarizes the desktop equity review of the "Full Upstream Access Architecture" (P3) proposed in v1.7.2. While the model demonstrates that an uncapped, scheduled fee-for-service (FFS) stream increases primary care supply and deflects hospital pressure, it carries inherent structural risks for Māori, Pacific peoples, rural communities, and high-deprivation populations. 

An uncapped FFS model, if implemented without strict constraints, risks exacerbating inequities. Therefore, the "controlled" aspect of the "uncapped but controlled" thesis must be explicitly designed around Te Tiriti o Waitangi principles and proactive equity protections.

## Key Equity Risks & Structural Mitigations

### 1. The Risk of "Cream-Skimming" (Options / Kōwhiringa)
**Risk:** An uncapped FFS stream strongly incentivizes high-volume, low-complexity consultations. Providers may establish clinics in affluent areas or prioritize simpler cases, leaving complex, multimorbid, or hard-to-reach patients (disproportionately Māori and Pacific) under-served.
**Mitigation:** The architecture must retain a strong **Capitation Base (F01)** for population responsibility. Furthermore, access to the uncapped FFS tier must be conditional on **Place-Based Accountability Contracts (F12)**, requiring providers to demonstrate service to the entire enrolled population, including outreach and management of complex conditions (D04, D07).

### 2. Co-Payment Exclusion (Equity / Ōritetanga)
**Risk:** If the scheduled benefit does not cover the full cost of care, providers will charge co-payments. High co-payments are a well-documented barrier to access for high-need and low-income populations, leading to delayed care and preventable hospitalizations (D09).
**Mitigation:** The model requires strong **Copayment Protection Strength (F07)**. The architecture must mandate zero-fee or highly subsidized tiers for high-need groups (e.g., CSC holders, children, targeted geographic areas) as a strict condition for providers to claim the FFS schedule. 

### 3. Rural Access Constraints (Equity / Ōritetanga)
**Risk:** Rural areas face absolute supply shortages. A pure FFS model relies on volume, which is difficult to achieve in low-density areas. Existing rural loadings on capitation may be insufficient.
**Mitigation:** The architecture must incorporate a **Rurality Demand Modifier (D05)** into the scheduled FFS rates. This means paying a higher scheduled benefit for services delivered in designated rural areas to compensate for lower volumes and higher operating costs, effectively stimulating rural supply (S12).

### 4. Cultural Safety and Data Sovereignty (Active Protection & Partnership)
**Risk:** Rapidly scaling supply through non-traditional providers (e.g., telehealth, retail pharmacy) may fragment care and bypass culturally safe, Kaupapa Māori providers. Furthermore, the data generated by this new activity may not be visible to or governed by Māori.
**Mitigation:** FFS eligibility must be tied to **Safety Governance (G01)** and **Equity Program Strength (G10)**, requiring cultural safety accreditation and integration with local care networks. **Te Tiriti Governance Strength (G11)** must be applied to the National Primary Care Dataset (NPCD) to ensure Māori have sovereignty over how access and outcomes data are collected, analyzed, and used to direct resources.

## Conclusion and Next Steps
The proposed hybrid architecture is only viable if these equity mitigations are treated as load-bearing structural components, not optional add-ons. 

**Action for Substack Release:** The final recommendation posts must explicitly deploy these findings as "Fair Counterarguments" to ensure the policy proposal is robust, defensible, and committed to health equity.
