# Falsification and sensitivity register — v0.4.1

## Purpose

This register identifies evidence or model results that would weaken, overturn or substantially modify the working hypothesis.

## Core hypothesis

New Zealand may be optimising allocation within tightly constrained upstream sectors while inadvertently channeling growth into hospitals. A transaction-level, scope-enabled, demand-driven primary care benefits schedule may allow lower-cost primary, urgent and ambulance care to expand before unmet need becomes hospital demand.

## Falsification tests

| Test | What would support hypothesis | What would weaken hypothesis |
|---|---|---|
| Capitation marginal-supply test | Practices show weak ability/incentive to expand contacts under capitation without higher co-payments or non-capitation revenue. | Practices can expand access materially under reweighted capitation alone without financial stress or higher patient charges. |
| ACC stabiliser test | ACC revenue is material to primary care viability and appointment capacity. | ACC revenue is marginal or unrelated to supply decisions. |
| PHO transaction-cost test | Providers identify PHO contracting/payment intermediation as a real entry/expansion barrier. | PHOs demonstrably reduce transaction cost and improve supply expansion relative to direct payment. |
| Benefits-schedule supply test | Activity-sensitive payments increase clinically appropriate access and reduce unmet need. | Activity-sensitive payments mainly increase low-value activity, cost and fragmentation. |
| Hospital-flow test | Improved upstream access reduces lower-urgency ED use, avoidable admissions or ambulance conveyance pressure. | Hospital demand trends are not materially affected by upstream access improvements. |
| Scope-enabled supply test | Pharmacists, NPs, allied health and paramedics safely expand defined contact types. | Scope-enabled claiming adds fragmentation without improving access or safety. |
| Co-payment equity test | Calibrated co-payments preserve access for high-needs groups. | Co-payments deter necessary care or widen inequities. |
| KPI salience test | Top-tier primary care and ambulance KPIs improve management attention and resource allocation. | KPIs create gaming or reporting burden without capacity improvement. |

## Sensitivity parameters for modelling

- marginal supply elasticity by contact type;
- price elasticity by patient group;
- practice fixed-cost and marginal-cost structure;
- ACC revenue share by practice type;
- non-GP provider capacity and substitution rate;
- telehealth substitution/complementarity rate;
- unmet need conversion rate to ED/ambulance/admission;
- PHO transaction cost/pass-through delay;
- rural in-person loading level;
- co-payment cap and concession settings;
- audit/gaming detection rate;
- hospital cost per avoidable event.

## Equity red flags

A policy scenario should be considered unacceptable if it:

- reduces access for Māori, Pacific, rural, disabled, low-income or high-complexity patients;
- increases co-payment exposure for high-need groups;
- undermines kaupapa Māori or Pacific provider viability;
- improves aggregate access while worsening distribution;
- shifts risk to patients or providers without adequate public funding.
