# Devil's advocate review v1.7.1

## Alternative interpretation
A reasonable critic could argue that the current reform pathway is already moving toward the right hybrid architecture: better capitation weights, access targets, dataset development, urgent-care investment, digital access, PHO accountability and separate appropriations. On that view, the risk is not that New Zealand lacks reform, but that an additional uncapped fee-for-service stream could destabilise the reform programme.

## Strongest counterarguments

1. **Capitation reweighting plus better targets may be enough.** If the National Primary Care Dataset and access target create real accountability, marginal supply may improve through management pressure and better distribution rather than FFS.
2. **Place-based commissioning may be more important than benefits.** If whole-population responsibility is the core problem, activity claims may be the wrong first lever.
3. **Public or salaried primary care may solve rurality better than fee-for-service.** Rural access may require capital, housing, workforce and infrastructure rather than scheduled item payments.
4. **Co-payments are a serious equity risk.** A demand signal that works for middle-income patients may become a care barrier for Māori, Pacific, disabled, rural and deprived populations.
5. **Scope expansion may fragment care.** More provider types can add supply, but may weaken continuity unless designed around shared records, accountability and clinical governance.
6. **Hospital growth may reflect ageing, complexity and hospital-specific drivers.** Primary care access is important, but may not be the dominant driver of hospital pressure.
7. **The ACC analogy may overstate transferability.** Injury episodes have clearer causality, eligibility and treatment pathways than general primary care illness.

## What the project should concede
- The current reform pathway is not trivial.
- Formula reweighting is necessary, even if insufficient.
- Uncapped FFS without strong controls would be dangerous.
- Place-based accountability is not optional.
- Equity review should happen before public recommendation posts.
- A full implementation proposal requires data and consultation beyond the current package.

## Revised position after devil's advocate review
The public claim should be modest but firm:

> Current reforms may be necessary but insufficient. The next question is whether they change the supply game enough. A scheduled, rules-based, audited FFS component for eligible primary medical care should be considered as one part of a hybrid architecture, not as a standalone replacement for capitation or place-based commissioning.
