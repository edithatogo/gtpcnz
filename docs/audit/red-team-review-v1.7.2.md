# Red-team review v1.7.2

## Purpose
This review tries to break the argument before critics do. It focuses on policy, evidence, modelling, communications and implementation risk.

## Summary judgement
The core thesis is coherent and worth publishing as exploratory policy analysis, but the highest-risk claims are: uncapped scheduled fee-for-service, PHO/payment intermediation, ACC stabilisation, and hospital-deflection. These need careful wording, source-confidence labels, and repeated caveats that the model is not predictive.

## Major red-team risks

| Risk | Why it matters | Mitigation added in v1.7.2 |
|---|---|---|
| “Uncapped fee-for-service will blow the budget.” | The phrase uncapped is politically vulnerable. | Always pair with scheduled, rules-based, audited, clinically governed and place-accountable. |
| “This is a GP money grab.” | Opponents may personalise the argument as sector self-interest. | Emphasise scope-enabled supply: pharmacists, nurses, nurse practitioners, allied health and paramedics where appropriate. |
| “The current reforms already solve this.” | Current reform is more substantial than a capitation-only straw man. | Treat current reform pathway as comparator, not caricature. |
| “PHO critique is inaccurate.” | Some funding is already nationally mandated/centrally paid. | Narrow critique to pass-through, transparency, non-capitated funding, entry barriers and function-versus-intermediation. |
| “The model has seventy assumptions.” | Over-parameterisation can reduce credibility. | Add parameter tiering and identifiability map. |
| “Equity is an afterthought.” | Co-payments and FFS can worsen access for high-need groups. | Equity review brief, co-payment protections, place accountability and Māori/Pacific review gate. |
| “ACC analogy is flawed.” | Injury treatment payment is not the same as illness-based primary care. | Use ACC as a rules-based payment analogy only, not as a wholesale model. |
| “Provider gaming will outrun audit.” | Real risk under activity-sensitive payment. | Treat gaming controls, item rules and data observability as core design, not add-ons. |
| “Public release before OIA responses is premature.” | Missing documents may shift the analysis. | Publish as exploratory and keep OIA targets visible. |
| “RACMA is not the right sponsor.” | RACMA feedback suggests fit may be limited. | Remove RACMA from active engagement pathway; allow passive re-entry only if they come back. |

## Red-team conclusion
Proceed publicly, but only through the short-form posts, with appendices for depth. Do not claim implementation readiness, fiscal savings or empirical proof. Freeze new model expansion until stakeholder validation and OIA responses are available.
