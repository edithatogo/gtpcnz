# Research audit pack — New Zealand game-theory mapping v0.4.1

## 1. Audit question

Can the current mapping work demonstrate a clear, reviewable pathway from policy hypothesis to mapped game structure, model specification, evidence requirements and policy outputs?

## 2. Audit answer

Yes, at the conceptual stage. The repo now contains a structured game atlas, formalisation notes, policy brief, Substack draft, NZMJ methods annex, figure source, and code scaffold. The audit pack adds explicit traceability from:

- policy claim;
- mapped game;
- source or theory basis;
- assumption requiring testing;
- downstream artefact;
- proposed validation or falsification method.

## 3. Audit standard used

No single reporting standard governs a policy game map. The audit approach therefore triangulates across:

- evidence synthesis reporting principles for scoping reviews;
- empirical simulation reporting principles;
- agent-based model description standards;
- health economic evaluation reporting principles;
- policy-traceability practice.

The relevant formal standards for later stages are:

- PRISMA-ScR for the rapid scoping review and evidence map;
- STRESS for system dynamics, agent-based and empirical simulation reporting;
- ODD for the agent-based model description;
- CHEERS 2022 if the work becomes a formal economic evaluation.

## 5. Claim status classification

Claims should be labelled using the following levels:

- **Level 1: Publicly documented fact** — directly supported by official or peer-reviewed source.
- **Level 2: Established theory** — supported by microeconomics, game theory, transaction-cost economics or principal-agent theory.
- **Level 3: Plausible mechanism** — supported by theory and partial evidence, but not yet empirically demonstrated in this setting.
- **Level 4: Modelling assumption** — included for simulation testing, not asserted as fact.
- **Level 5: Policy option** — proposed architecture requiring feasibility, equity and fiscal analysis.

## 6. Current high-risk assumptions

The assumptions most in need of testing are:

1. ACC fee-for-service and programme payments materially stabilise general practice supply.
2. PHO intermediation materially increases transaction cost or market-entry barriers.
3. A demand-driven benefits schedule would expand supply rather than mainly increase low-value activity.
4. Co-payment calibration can preserve a demand signal without worsening inequity.
5. Non-GP providers can safely and materially expand primary care supply if payment eligibility follows scope and clinical governance.
6. Primary care and ambulance KPIs elevated to top-tier reporting would change management attention and funding behaviour.
7. Hospital growth is partly endogenous to constrained upstream access rather than only demographic, epidemiological or workforce-driven.

## 7. Audit conclusion

The mapping work is now sufficiently documented for internal RACMA-style scoping, stakeholder challenge and conversion into a review/modelling protocol. The next step is not more conceptual mapping alone; it is evidence extraction, stakeholder validation, OIA follow-up and model calibration.
