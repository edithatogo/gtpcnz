# Stakeholder validation plan — v0.4.1

## Purpose

The game map should be challenged by people who operate within each game layer. The purpose is not consensus; it is to identify missing mechanisms, false assumptions and politically infeasible options.

## Stakeholder groups

| Group | Validation role |
|---|---|
| General practice owners | Practice viability, marginal supply, enrolment and co-payment behaviour. |
| Salaried GPs and registrars | Workforce incentives, burnout, appointment supply and professional constraints. |
| Nurse practitioners and nurses | Scope-enabled supply, clinical governance and substitution/complementarity. |
| Pharmacists/pharmacist prescribers | Contact-type eligibility, medicine management and access expansion. |
| Allied health providers | MSK, mental health, rehabilitation and chronic care contact types. |
| Māori health providers | Equity, Te Tiriti, trust, locality, kaupapa Māori delivery and risk of direct-payment architecture. |
| Pacific providers | Equity, access, culturally specific care and funding architecture risks. |
| Rural providers | Local in-person viability, telehealth substitution and rural loading design. |
| PHO leaders | PHO value, intermediation, contracting, data and population health functions. |
| Health NZ commissioning/operations | Budget architecture, hospital pressure and commissioning constraints. |
| Ministry of Health policy officials | Stewardship, regulation, funding settings and accountability architecture. |
| Treasury officials or former officials | Fiscal architecture, demand-driven expenditure and value-for-money tests. |
| ACC officials or former officials | Injury/medical funding interface and primary care supply effects. |
| Ambulance providers/paramedics | Hear-and-treat, treat-and-refer, response KPIs and ED conveyance incentives. |
| Hospital/ED leaders | Downstream effects of upstream access constraints. |
| Consumer/patient advocates | Co-payment, choice, access, digital divide and equity risks. |

## Validation questions

1. Does this game layer reflect how decisions are actually made?
2. Which players or incentives are missing?
3. Which payoff is overstated or understated?
4. What evidence would convince you the mechanism is real?
5. What evidence would convince you the mechanism is wrong?
6. What unintended consequences would the proposed benefits schedule create?
7. Which contact types could safely be opened to a broader provider range?
8. Which contact types must remain tightly governed or restricted?
9. What co-payment design would be unacceptable?
10. What KPIs would improve accountability without creating gaming?

## Method

- Initial 45–60 minute semi-structured interviews.
- One cross-sector validation workshop.
- Separate equity-focused review with Māori and Pacific voices before public claims harden.
- Written feedback option for politically sensitive participants.
- Thematic coding against game layers, assumptions and falsification tests.

## Outputs

- Stakeholder feedback matrix.
- Revised game map.
- Assumption status update.
- Policy feasibility note.
- Equity and implementation risk register.
