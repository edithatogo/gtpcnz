# Co-payments: demand signal or equity failure?

One of the hardest questions in primary care funding is whether patients should pay something at the point of care.

Economists often see co-payments as a demand signal. If a service is free at the point of use, people may use more of it, including some care that is convenient but not clinically necessary. A modest co-payment can make people think twice. It can also support provider viability in a system where public subsidy does not cover full cost.

But health care is not a normal consumer good.

The person deciding whether to seek care often does not know whether the problem is minor, serious, time-critical or safely ignored. The person most deterred by price may be the person with the highest need and the least money. A co-payment can therefore become an equity failure mechanism.

So the question is not whether co-payments are good or bad. The question is what job they are being asked to do.

In the model I am proposing, co-payments are not the main funding source. They are a calibrated demand signal sitting alongside a public benefit for defined primary care contacts. That means the public system would subsidise the types of care it wants people to use earlier: urgent primary care, chronic disease review, medicines optimisation, nurse practitioner care, pharmacist care, physiotherapy assessment, wound care, mental health support, rural in-person care and ambulance treat-and-refer pathways.

The co-payment would then be designed around equity.

Children, high-needs groups, Community Services Card holders and some preventive contacts might have no or very low co-payments. Essential contacts could have capped co-payments. Higher public benefits could apply for rurality, deprivation, disability, Maori and Pacific peoples, and multimorbidity. Some contacts could prohibit balance billing altogether.

This is different from saying “let the market decide.” It is saying: let consumers choose eligible lower-cost care, but do not let price become the reason high-needs people miss out.

A good co-payment design should answer five questions.

First, which contacts should have no co-payment because access matters more than demand restraint?

Second, which contacts can have a modest co-payment because the risk of harmful underuse is lower?

Third, how do we protect people with frequent need, chronic disease or disability from cumulative cost?

Fourth, how do we monitor whether people are delaying care because of cost?

Fifth, how do we make fees transparent enough that patients can choose and policymakers can see what is happening?

The worst possible version of this reform would be a public subsidy that simply allows providers to charge more. The best version would be a public subsidy that expands lower-cost care, protects high-needs patients and makes pricing visible.

Co-payments can be a useful part of the game. They cannot be allowed to become the way the system rations care.
