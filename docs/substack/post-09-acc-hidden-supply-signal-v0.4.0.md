# Post 09 draft: Is ACC hiding New Zealand's primary care supply problem?

**Version:** v0.4.0  
**Status:** Draft

New Zealand has two primary care funding games running at the same time.

For ordinary illness, general practice is funded substantially through capitation: a fixed payment linked to enrolled patients. Capitation has real strengths. It supports continuity, enrolled-population responsibility and a way of weighting funding toward need.

But injury care is different. ACC-funded care has always retained a much stronger activity signal. If someone has an injury, the primary care system receives a payment linked to that episode of care.

That raises an uncomfortable question:

> Is ACC partially stabilising a primary care market that the ordinary illness funding model is otherwise constraining?

This is not an argument against ACC. It is the opposite. It is an argument that ACC should not be analysed in isolation from the primary care market it helps keep viable.

If a practice receives activity-linked ACC revenue, that can support the workforce, rooms, systems and appointment capacity also used for non-injury patients. If ACC payments are tightened without replacing that activity signal elsewhere, the effect may not be limited to injury care. It may reduce the viability of local primary care supply more broadly.

That is why this problem belongs partly with Treasury. It is a whole-system fiscal question. A saving inside ACC could create a cost inside Health NZ or hospitals if it removes appointment supply that was previously absorbing need in the community.

The policy question is not whether ACC should fund everything. It is whether New Zealand has accidentally allowed ACC to become one of the few remaining activity-sensitive stabilisers in primary care.

If so, reforming ACC in isolation risks pulling the rug from under a system that is already short of appointment capacity.
