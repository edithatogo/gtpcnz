# Why primary care funding is not just about more money

When people hear that primary care needs more funding, it can sound like a sector asking for a larger slice of the pie. That is not the argument I want to make.

The deeper issue is how money flows, what behaviour it rewards, and what kinds of care it makes viable.

A funding model is not just an accounting method. It is a set of rules. It tells providers whether it is viable to take on new patients, offer urgent appointments, invest in nurses and pharmacists, maintain rural in-person care, provide longer complex consultations, or shift work into telehealth.

New Zealand has relied heavily on capitation: a fixed payment per enrolled patient. Capitation has real strengths. It supports continuity, population health, budget predictability and some equity goals. But if it becomes too dominant, it can also weaken the payment signal for the next clinically necessary piece of work.

That matters because patient need does not disappear when primary care is constrained. It shows up somewhere else: delayed care, higher co-payments, closed books, ambulance calls, emergency departments and hospital admissions.

This is the funding architecture problem.

The uncomfortable possibility is that New Zealand is tightly managing lower-cost primary care and ambulance activity in a way that unintentionally buys hospital growth by default.

The solution is not simply to copy Australia's fee-for-service model. Fee-for-service has its own problems. It can reward activity without necessarily rewarding value. But fee-for-service does something capitation struggles to do: it creates a marginal payment for the next service.

The better question is not capitation or fee-for-service. It is what kind of blended architecture lets the right care happen in the right place, by the right provider, before the hospital becomes the only politically unavoidable option.

That is the primary care funding game.
