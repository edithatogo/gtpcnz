# The New Zealand primary care funding game

Sometimes a health system can do the rational thing in every individual corner and still produce the wrong overall result.

That is what I think may be happening in New Zealand.

The government tries to control costs. Health NZ tries to manage hospitals. PHOs defend their role. Practices protect viability. Patients try to get care when they can. Ambulance providers choose pathways that are safe, funded and defensible. Hospitals ask for rescue when pressure becomes unbearable.

None of those choices is irrational.

But together they may create a game where we tightly manage primary care and ambulance funding, then wonder why hospitals keep growing.

## The hospital always becomes visible

A hospital under pressure is visible. Emergency department waits are visible. Cancelled surgery is visible. Workforce shortages are visible. Deficits are visible. Ministers, boards and executives cannot ignore them.

Primary care access failure is different. It is dispersed. It shows up as a practice with closed books, a longer wait for an appointment, a patient who delays care, a higher co-payment, a rural person who uses telehealth when they really need examination, or a whanau that gives up until the problem becomes urgent.

Eventually some of that unmet need reaches the hospital.

By then it is visible.

And because it is visible, it gets funded.

That is the game.

## Capitation helps, but it can also constrain

New Zealand's general practice funding model is built around capitation: a fixed payment for enrolled patients. Capitation is not a bad idea. It supports population responsibility, continuity and equity.

But capitation has a marginal-supply problem.

Once a patient is enrolled, an extra appointment still costs time, staff, rooms, documentation, risk and follow-up. If the funding model does not adequately pay for that extra clinically necessary work, the system rations in other ways.

It rations through waiting.
It rations through co-payments.
It rations through closed books.
It rations through shorter appointments.
It rations through telehealth substitution.

A better capitation formula may distribute money more fairly. That matters. But it does not automatically create more appointments.

## The missing idea: a primary care benefit

The alternative is not uncontrolled fee-for-service.

The alternative is to define the types of primary care contacts the public system wants to support, then allow accredited providers to generate those contacts within scope and clinical governance rules.

That could include GPs, nurse practitioners, pharmacists, nurses, physiotherapists, paramedics, kaupapa Maori providers, Pacific providers and others, depending on the contact type.

In other words: funding should follow the clinically appropriate contact, not just the traditional provider category.

## Ambulance is part of the same game

Ambulance is often treated as transport to hospital. But ambulance can also be access infrastructure.

If paramedics can safely assess, treat, refer, connect patients to urgent primary care or arrange community follow-up, then ambulance can prevent hospital demand.

But that only happens if the rules support it.

If the safe, funded, measured and defensible pathway is conveyance to ED, then ED becomes the default destination.

## PHOs: function or friction?

PHOs may do valuable things: population health, quality improvement, locality support, equity work, data support and community relationships.

But those functions are not the same as payment intermediation.

A key question is whether public primary care funding should have to pass through a PHO-like structure before providers can generate eligible care.

Maybe some PHO functions should stay. Maybe some should be strengthened. Maybe some should be replaced.

But the payment gateway should be tested, not assumed.

## The simple version

New Zealand may be tightly managing the cheaper parts of the system and accidentally buying growth in the expensive part.

The policy question is not only:

> How should capitation be reweighted?

It is:

> What rules would let safe, lower-cost care expand before unmet need becomes hospital demand?

That is the primary care funding game.
