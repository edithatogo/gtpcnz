# Fee-for-service, capitation and programme funding: the 101

There are three basic ways governments commonly pay for primary care.

The first is fee-for-service. A provider does something, and a payment is made. This is simple and powerful. It supports supply because the next clinically necessary appointment is funded. The risk is that it can reward volume, fragmentation and low-value activity if poorly designed.

The second is capitation. A provider or practice receives a fixed payment per enrolled patient. This supports population accountability, continuity and budget control. The risk is that once a patient is enrolled, extra work may not be matched by extra payment. In a busy system, the rational response can be waiting times, closed books, shorter consultations or higher patient charges.

The third is programme funding. This pays for particular activities or populations: chronic disease reviews, immunisations, care coordination, rural care or other targeted services. The risk is administrative complexity and narrow eligibility.

None of these models is perfect. Each solves one problem and creates another.

A sensible system blends them. Capitation can fund continuity and population responsibility. Programme funding can support planned care. Activity-sensitive payments can support timely access, procedures, urgent care and complex consultations.

But there is a deeper issue. In New Zealand, capitation is not just a payment method. It is part of a wider administrative architecture involving enrolment, PHOs, contracts and constrained public funding. That architecture can affect who enters the market, who can claim, who can expand supply and who is allowed to generate funded activity.

That is why reweighting capitation may be necessary but insufficient.
