# A Primary Care Benefits Schedule for New Zealand

What if New Zealand treated primary care access more like a public benefit than a fixed local contract?

That does not mean an uncontrolled free-for-all. It means defining eligible primary care contact types and allowing accredited providers to claim public subsidies when those contacts are delivered safely and appropriately.

This could include acute minor illness, chronic disease reviews, medicines management, wound care, musculoskeletal assessment, mental health brief interventions, urgent after-hours care, rural in-person care, paramedic treat-and-refer pathways and nurse practitioner-led care.

The provider would not always need to be a GP. The test should be clinical scope, competence and governance. A pharmacist should be able to deliver funded medicines-management activity. A physiotherapist should be able to deliver funded musculoskeletal assessment. A paramedic should be able to deliver funded treat-and-refer care. A nurse practitioner should be able to provide funded urgent and chronic care within scope.

The payment would be rules-based. The public subsidy would attach to the contact type. The patient co-payment would act as a demand signal, with strong protections for children, high-needs groups, rural communities and people with multimorbidity.

Capitation would not disappear. It would remain for continuity, enrolment and population health. But it would no longer be the main mechanism determining whether a patient can access care.

This is not ring-fencing primary care into a fixed pool. It is almost the opposite. It is making primary care benefits open to demand, but constrained by rules at the transaction level: eligible contact type, eligible provider, clinical scope, safety standards, data reporting and co-payment rules.

The policy question is whether this would allow lower-cost care to grow before need is forced into hospitals.
