# Who should be allowed to generate primary care supply?

When people hear “primary care,” they often think “GP appointments.” That is understandable. General practice is central to primary care and GPs are essential for undifferentiated illness, diagnostic complexity, continuity, prescribing, risk management and clinical governance.

But primary care is bigger than GP appointments.

A person may need a medicines review, a wound dressing, a musculoskeletal assessment, a mental health brief intervention, a diabetes foot check, a vaccination, a cervical screen, an urgent same-day assessment, a paramedic treat-and-refer pathway, or a nurse practitioner review. Some of those contacts need a GP. Some do not. Some are best delivered by a multidisciplinary team.

A funding model should recognise that.

At the moment, primary care funding is often tied to traditional practice structures, enrolment rules and professional assumptions. That can make sense for continuity, but it can also restrict supply. If public subsidy can only reliably flow through a narrow model, then the system has created an access bottleneck before clinical need is even considered.

The better principle is simple:

> Funding should follow clinically appropriate contact types, delivered by accredited providers acting within scope.

That does not mean removing safety constraints. It means using the right constraints.

Prescribing regulation should determine who can prescribe. Professional scope should determine who can assess and treat. Credentialing should determine who can deliver a pathway. Clinical governance should determine escalation, supervision, documentation and audit. Data reporting should make activity visible. Quality monitoring should detect problems.

But the payment model should not add unnecessary professional barriers on top of those rules.

A pharmacist may be the right provider for medicines optimisation or stable repeat prescribing review. A physiotherapist may be the right first contact for many musculoskeletal problems. A nurse practitioner may manage acute and chronic care independently. A paramedic may safely treat and refer some patients without conveyance to hospital. A kaupapa Maori provider may reach whanau that traditional clinic-based care has missed.

The aim is not to replace GPs. It is to stop using GPs as the only gateway to every kind of subsidised primary care activity.

If we want primary care supply to grow, we need to let more of the primary care workforce generate clinically appropriate activity. That is not anti-doctor. It is pro-access, pro-team and pro-system.
