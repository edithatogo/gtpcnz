# Decision-maker summary v1.7.2

## The short argument
New Zealand is already reforming primary care. That matters. The question is whether the current reform pathway changes the strategic game enough.

The concern is not simply that primary care needs more money. The concern is that lower-cost upstream care can be tightly controlled while hospital demand remains visible, urgent and fundable. If primary, urgent and ambulance care cannot safely expand, unmet need may appear later as emergency department pressure, ambulance conveyance, admissions or more complex illness.

## Current reform pathway
Current reform includes capitation reweighting, access targets, data infrastructure, urgent and after-hours care, digital access, PHO accountability and separate appropriations. This should be treated as the comparator, not dismissed.

## Red-team concession
A reasonable critic could argue that current reforms may be sufficient. The work should therefore be framed as a testable question:

> Do current reforms remove the marginal supply constraint, or do they mainly improve allocation, measurement and accountability within a still-constrained upstream system?

## What may still be missing
- A marginal supply signal for eligible primary medical activity.
- Place-based accountability to prevent cherry-picking.
- Transparent PHO pass-through and payment intermediation analysis.
- Strong co-payment protections.
- Scope-enabled supply across appropriate provider groups.
- Top-tier primary care, urgent care and ambulance accountability.
- Data strong enough to detect gaming, inequity, fragmentation and unmet need.

## Proposed hybrid architecture
Capitation for continuity and population responsibility; an uncapped, scheduled, rules-based fee-for-service stream for eligible primary medical care; place-based accountability; provider eligibility by scope and clinical governance; audit and data controls; equity protections; urgent/ambulance integration; and explicit safeguards against cherry-picking, low-value activity and inequity.

## What not to conclude yet
This work does not prove fiscal savings, emergency department reduction or exact supply effects. It is a structured policy hypothesis, with modelling and validation artefacts ready for critique.

## Five tests
1. Does activity-sensitive payment increase useful upstream supply?
2. Does unmet primary care need convert into emergency department, ambulance or hospital demand?
3. Does Accident Compensation Corporation activity funding stabilise upstream provider capacity?
4. Does PHO intermediation create friction or pass-through opacity?
5. Can scope-enabled providers safely expand supply without fragmenting care?

## Engagement path
Do not pursue RACMA as an active sponsor. Publish, validate, request documents, and let interested organisations come back if they see value.
