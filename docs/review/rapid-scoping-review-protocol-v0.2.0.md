# Rapid scoping review protocol: primary care funding architecture

## Purpose

To map evidence on primary care payment architecture, provider incentives, market entry, co-payments, administrative intermediaries, ambulance/prehospital alternatives and hospital-avoidance outcomes, with specific relevance to Aotearoa New Zealand and Australia.

## Review type

Rapid scoping review with realist/mechanism coding. This is more appropriate than a narrow intervention-effectiveness review because the question is architecture and mechanism, not only whether one payment intervention increased one service outcome.

## Primary question

How do primary care payment models and administrative architectures affect access, supply, equity, provider behaviour, co-payments, market entry and hospital demand?

## Secondary questions

1. What mechanisms explain under-provision under capitation and over-provision under fee-for-service?
2. What evidence supports blended payment models?
3. What evidence exists on direct benefit/claims systems for primary care contacts?
4. What is known about co-payments as demand signals and access barriers?
5. What is known about pharmacist, nurse practitioner, allied health and paramedic-generated primary care activity?
6. What is known about administrative intermediaries, transaction costs and market entry?
7. What evidence links primary care access or urgent/prehospital alternatives to ED and hospital demand?

## Include

- Primary care, community care, urgent care, general practice, family medicine, outpatient ambulatory care, ambulance/prehospital treat-and-refer.
- Payment models: capitation, fee-for-service, blended payments, bundled payments, pay-for-performance, direct claims, benefits schedules, co-payments, subsidies.
- Outcomes: activity, supply, waiting time, open/closed books, enrolment, provider entry/exit, co-payments, quality, continuity, equity, ED use, ambulatory-sensitive hospitalisation, ambulance conveyance/non-conveyance, cost.
- Study types: systematic reviews, trials, quasi-experimental studies, observational studies, simulation modelling, economic experiments, policy reviews, grey literature.
- Jurisdictions: OECD countries, with priority for NZ, Australia, UK, Canada, US, Netherlands and Nordic systems.

## Exclude

- Purely hospital payment models unless directly linked to primary care substitution.
- Inpatient-only DRG/payment reform without upstream care component.
- Opinion pieces without a clearly stated mechanism or evidence basis, unless used for policy context.

## Draft search string

```text
(primary care OR general practice OR family medicine OR ambulatory care OR community care OR urgent care OR prehospital OR ambulance)
AND
(capitation OR fee-for-service OR blended payment OR mixed payment OR bundled payment OR pay-for-performance OR co-payment OR copayment OR benefits schedule OR direct claims OR provider payment OR payment model OR funding architecture)
AND
(access OR supply OR waiting time OR enrolment OR closed books OR provider behaviour OR market entry OR workforce OR equity OR hospitalisation OR emergency department OR avoidable admission OR ambulatory sensitive OR transaction cost)
```

## Extraction fields

- Citation;
- country/setting;
- population;
- payment model;
- administrative architecture;
- provider types;
- co-payment settings;
- outcomes;
- measured mechanism;
- effect direction;
- certainty/quality assessment;
- relevance to NZ;
- relevance to Australia;
- modelling parameter implication;
- notes.
