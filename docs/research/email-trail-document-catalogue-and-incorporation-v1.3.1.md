# Email-trail incorporation plus uncapped FFS clarification — v1.3.1

This release incorporates the RACMA email-trail document audit and Dylan's subsequent clarification that the intended policy is **not** merely a ring-fenced primary care budget. The proposal is to remove the fixed global cap on eligible primary care activity and migrate part of the system to an **ACC-like, rules-based fee-for-service stream for medical primary care**, while retaining capitation for population accountability and adding place-based safeguards.

## Key correction

The earlier phrase **National Primary Care Benefits Schedule + place-based population accountability** remains useful, but it was too easy to read as a capped programme. The intended architecture is more specific:

> **An uncapped, demand-led primary medical benefit stream, paid fee-for-service for eligible medical primary care contacts, with capitation retained for baseline continuity and place-based accountability retained for equity, rurality, outreach and hard-to-reach populations.**

## What “uncapped” means here

It does **not** mean no rules, no prices, no audit and no clinical governance. It means:

- no fixed annual global pool that runs out or suppresses eligible activity;
- a published schedule of eligible claimable contact types and item values;
- payment generated when an eligible patient receives an eligible service from an eligible provider;
- item-level controls, clinical-necessity rules, scope-of-practice rules and audit replacing global budget caps;
- co-payment settings used as part of demand calibration, with equity protections.

## What remains capped or controlled

- Item prices / public contribution rates.
- Eligible service definitions.
- Provider credentialing and scope.
- Clinical necessity, duration and frequency.
- Prior approval for some higher-risk/high-cost services.
- Audit, unusual billing detection and low-value activity controls.
- Co-payment protections for children, Community Services Card holders, high-need groups and other priority populations.

## Relationship to ACC

The analogy is ACC's treatment payment architecture: ACC can pay under Cost of Treatment Regulations, contracts or purchase orders. Services must be clinically necessary, suitable, documented, within provider scope and, where required, pre-approved. The proposed primary care stream would adapt that **rules-based, activity-sensitive logic** to primary medical care, while recognising that ordinary health need is broader than covered injury.

## Relationship to capitation

Capitation should not disappear. It should be moved into its best role:

- baseline continuity;
- enrolled population responsibility;
- care coordination;
- proactive management of high-need groups;
- practice/team viability;
- rural/place-based obligations.

But capitation should no longer be the main mechanism that determines whether an additional clinically necessary appointment is financially viable.

## Updated formulation

> **Remove the fixed cap on eligible primary medical care activity. Keep capitation for continuity and population responsibility. Add ACC-style fee-for-service for eligible medical primary care contacts. Use place-based accountability, co-payment protections, scope rules and audit to manage equity, quality and fiscal risk.**
