# Stakeholder validation pack v0.5.0

## Purpose

Test whether the mapped games reflect real-world incentives and constraints experienced by primary care providers, PHOs/locality organisations, ambulance providers, funders, Māori/Pacific providers, rural communities and hospital leaders.

This is validation of mechanisms, not lobbying for a predetermined option.

## Core validation questions

1. Does the game map omit important actors or incentives?
2. Are the mapped equilibria recognisable in practice?
3. Which claims are strongest, weakest or wrong?
4. Where would a demand-driven primary care benefits schedule improve access?
5. Where would it create low-value activity, fragmentation, inequity or safety risks?
6. Which PHO functions are essential and which payment-gateway functions could be optionalised or replaced?
7. How material is ACC activity funding to practice viability?
8. What ambulance alternatives to ED conveyance are feasible and safe?
9. Which provider types could safely generate which primary care contact types?
10. Which KPIs should be top-tier, and what balancing measures are needed?

## Interview guide A — general practice and primary care providers

- What currently determines whether you can offer more appointments?
- Which funding streams make marginal activity viable?
- Which contacts are under-provided because they are poorly funded or administratively difficult?
- How do capitation, co-payment, ACC and programme funding interact in practice?
- Do PHOs reduce or increase administrative burden for your service?
- Would a national benefits schedule change your capacity, team mix or willingness to expand?
- Which non-GP providers could safely absorb additional demand under governance?
- What safeguards would be needed to prevent churn, fragmentation or unsafe substitution?

## Interview guide B — PHOs/locality/iwi/Māori/Pacific providers

- Which functions do PHOs/locality entities perform that are not visible from payment flows?
- Which functions should not be lost under any direct-claims model?
- Where does payment intermediation add value, and where does it add friction?
- Would direct provider benefits undermine or support kaupapa Māori/Pacific/locality models?
- What equity protections would be non-negotiable?
- How should population-health and outreach activities be funded if activity benefits expand?

## Interview guide C — ambulance and urgent care

- When does ambulance substitute for primary/urgent care access?
- What barriers prevent hear-and-treat, treat-and-refer or alternative destination pathways?
- How do Health NZ and ACC funding arrangements affect operational decisions?
- What data are available on non-conveyance safety, recontact, ED conveyance and handover delay?
- Which ambulance KPIs should be top-tier and which balancing measures are needed?

## Interview guide D — funders, Treasury, Ministry, Health NZ, ACC

- Where are primary care, ambulance and hospital pressures visible in decision-making?
- Which budget rules amplify or reduce hospital-rescue dynamics?
- Is unmet primary care need measured in a way that informs resource allocation?
- What fiscal safeguards would be necessary for a demand-driven benefits schedule?
- What would make a benefits schedule unacceptable or infeasible?
- How should cross-funder externalities between ACC and Health NZ be modelled?

## Interview guide E — consumers and communities

- What happens when timely primary care is not available?
- How do co-payments, travel, trust, provider type and digital access affect choices?
- What care would you accept from pharmacists, nurses, nurse practitioners, allied health or paramedics?
- What would make care feel safe, continuous and culturally appropriate?

## Analysis framework

Code interviews against:

- marginal supply;
- transaction cost;
- market entry;
- access pathway substitution;
- equity and trust;
- professional scope;
- ambulance alternatives;
- budget visibility;
- KPI salience;
- gaming/safety risks;
- policy feasibility.

## Ethics and governance note

If findings will be published academically, seek ethics approval before interviews. If engagement is framed as policy consultation, document consent, confidentiality, conflicts and data handling clearly.
