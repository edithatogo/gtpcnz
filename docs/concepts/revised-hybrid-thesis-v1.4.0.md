# Revised hybrid thesis v1.4.0

## The corrected policy thesis

The recommended architecture is:

> **Capitation + uncapped eligible primary medical fee-for-service + place-based population accountability + urgent/ambulance integration + provider-scope flexibility.**

This is not a proposal for an uncontrolled market. It is a proposal to remove hard global caps on eligible primary medical activity while controlling the system at the transaction, provider, clinical-governance, data, co-payment and place-accountability levels.

## What is being capped, and what is not

- **Do not cap** the total annual volume of eligible primary medical activity through a fixed envelope that suppresses marginal supply.
- **Do cap or schedule** public contribution rates for defined items.
- **Do define** clinical eligibility, documentation, duration/frequency rules, scope-of-practice rules, pre-approval thresholds and audit triggers.
- **Do retain** capitation for continuity, enrolment, proactive care, care coordination and baseline viability.
- **Do require** place-based accountability so providers cannot simply chase easy, low-complexity activity.

## Why the email trail changes the analysis

The email trail forced three improvements.

First, New Zealand already has separate hospital/specialist and primary/community/public/population appropriations. The problem is not a single undifferentiated hospital pot. The problem is a strategic game where hospital demand is visible and politically urgent while unmet upstream need is dispersed, delayed and only partly measured.

Second, the current reform pathway is more substantial than a simple capitation-reweighting exercise. It includes capitation reweighting, access targets, a National Primary Care Dataset, performance payments, urgent/after-hours expansion, digital 24/7 care, workforce initiatives and PHO accountability work. That must be treated as the comparator.

Third, any uncapped fee-for-service stream needs anti-cherry-picking guardrails. The missing piece is not just “more supply”; it is “more supply with responsibility for whole populations.”

## Why capitation still matters

Capitation is useful for:

- continuity;
- responsibility for enrolled populations;
- proactive and preventive care;
- team-based non-visit work;
- equity weighting;
- baseline viability.

But capitation is weak at funding the next clinically necessary contact. Once the patient is enrolled, the practice receives a fixed payment. The next appointment creates marginal cost. If marginal revenue is low, the system rations through wait times, closed books, shorter appointments, higher co-payments or substitution to urgent care, ambulance and emergency departments.

## Why ACC is the better analogy

The Accident Compensation Corporation is not a perfect model for primary care. It funds injury, not general illness. But it demonstrates a crucial architecture:

- defined eligible services;
- scheduled public contributions;
- provider qualification and scope rules;
- documentation requirements;
- contracts or regulations;
- pre-approval where needed;
- audit and utilisation control;
- co-payments where the public contribution does not cover the full fee.

That is closer to the proposed primary medical stream than a generic “uncapped funding” idea.

## Updated recommendation

New Zealand should test a National Primary Medical Benefits Schedule for eligible medical contacts, initially for defined high-value contact types such as urgent primary medical care, complex consultations, rural in-person care, minor procedures, follow-up after ambulance non-conveyance, and care that prevents emergency department escalation.

This stream should sit alongside reweighted capitation and place-based commissioning. It should be demand-led within rules, not budget-capped at the envelope level.
