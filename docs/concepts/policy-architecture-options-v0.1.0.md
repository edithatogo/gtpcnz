# Policy architecture options

## Option A: Status quo with capitation reweighting

Capitation remains dominant. The formula is reweighted to better account for age, sex, deprivation, rurality and multimorbidity. PHO-mediated architecture remains largely intact.

**Strengths:** improves allocation fairness; administratively familiar.  
**Risks:** may leave marginal supply constraint unresolved; may not reduce closed books, waiting times or hospital growth.

## Option B: Reweighted capitation plus bounded access payment

Capitation remains foundational, but a defined activity-sensitive payment is added for timely access, complex care, urgent episodes, in-person rural care and procedures.

**Strengths:** blended model; easier transition.  
**Risks:** may remain too constrained if the payment sits inside a fixed envelope or PHO-mediated gateway.

## Option C: National Primary Care Benefits Schedule

A demand-driven public benefit is attached to defined contact types. Accredited providers claim directly through a national platform. Co-payments and equity protections shape demand. Capitation remains for continuity and population health but is no longer the main access mechanism.

**Strengths:** removes fixed-envelope supply constraint; supports consumer choice; supports multidisciplinary supply; improves market entry.  
**Risks:** fiscal exposure, low-value activity, fragmentation, equity risks from co-payments.

## Option D: Primary care plus ambulance hospital-avoidance benefits

Extends Option C to include ambulance/prehospital contact types such as hear-and-treat, treat-and-refer, alternative destination pathways and paramedic-led urgent care where safe.

**Strengths:** explicitly models primary care and ambulance as lower-cost alternatives to ED/hospital flow.  
**Risks:** safety and accountability require careful design.

## Option E: Independent primary care payment authority

Creates an independent pricing/payment body for primary care and prehospital benefits, analogous in principle to independent hospital pricing or the Australian GP Incentives Review's proposal for independent pricing of primary care payments.

**Strengths:** depoliticises pricing and improves transparency.  
**Risks:** new bureaucracy; may duplicate existing agencies.

## Preferred initial policy package

A staged hybrid:

1. Immediate transparency/OIA and public accounting of capitation, PHO pass-through, ACC spillover and ambulance performance.
2. Model Option A vs B vs C vs D.
3. Pilot a Primary Care Benefits Schedule for defined contact types in one or more regions, with high-needs and rural weighting.
4. Lift primary care and ambulance KPIs into top-tier accountability.
5. Evaluate hospital avoidance, equity, market entry and fiscal effects.
