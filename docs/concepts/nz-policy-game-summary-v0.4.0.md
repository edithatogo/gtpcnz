# NZ policy game summary

## The shortest version

Stop tightly rationing primary care and ambulance activity, then wondering why hospitals grow.

## The slightly more formal version

New Zealand may be optimising allocation within constrained upstream sectors while failing to remove the constraints that channel unmet need into hospitals.

Capitation reweighting can improve fairness between enrolled populations. It does not necessarily generate more appointments, more urgent care, more rural in-person supply, more paramedic treat-and-refer options, or more pharmacist/nurse/allied-health-led primary care contacts.

## The policy move

Create a National Primary Care Benefits Schedule: public subsidies for defined contact types, claimable by accredited providers acting within scope, with co-payment calibration, data reporting, audit and clinical governance.

This is not classic fee-for-service and not classic capitation. It is a hybrid game designed to improve supply without abandoning population accountability.

## Why game theory helps

Game theory helps because the current problem is not just a payment formula. It is a repeated interaction between actors who are responding rationally to distorted incentives:

- Ministers respond to visible hospital pressure.
- Health NZ responds to targets, deficits and direct hospital delivery obligations.
- Providers respond to marginal revenue, workload and risk.
- Patients respond to co-payments, waiting times, trust and convenience.
- Ambulance responds to safety, response targets and available alternatives.
- PHOs respond to their role as intermediaries.
- Professional groups respond to scope and income risks.

No actor needs to behave badly for the system to produce a bad equilibrium.
