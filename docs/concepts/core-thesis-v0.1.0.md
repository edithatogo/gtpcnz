# Core thesis

## One-sentence version

New Zealand may be tightly managing primary care and ambulance activity in a way that unintentionally buys hospital growth by default.

## More formal version

Current reform appears focused on improving allocation within existing primary care funding architecture, especially through capitation reweighting. That may improve equity of distribution, but it does not necessarily remove constraints on marginal supply, market entry, professional substitution, urgent access or rural in-person care. If upstream lower-cost sectors remain constrained, unmet need may shift into higher-cost hospital settings, where demand is more visible, politically salient and difficult to refuse.

## Policy hypothesis

A demand-driven, rules-based Primary Care Benefits Schedule could allow lower-cost primary, urgent, community and prehospital contacts to expand in response to patient need. The schedule would define eligible contact types, eligible provider scopes, payment rules, co-payment settings and quality safeguards. It would not be ring-fenced by a fixed total envelope; it would be constrained at the transaction level.

## Why this differs from simple blended funding

Standard blended funding asks how to combine capitation, fee-for-service and programme payments. This project asks a deeper architecture question:

> Should public primary care benefits be tied to enrolment, PHO intermediation and fixed-envelope commissioning, or should they be portable, consumer-triggered and generated by a wider range of accredited providers within scope?

## Proposed architecture

1. **Baseline capitation:** retained for continuity, population-health responsibility and baseline viability.
2. **Primary Care Benefits Schedule:** public subsidy for defined primary care contact types.
3. **Scope-based provider eligibility:** GPs, nurse practitioners, pharmacists, paramedics, physiotherapists, mental health practitioners and other providers can generate activity where clinically appropriate.
4. **Co-payment as demand signal:** consumers retain agency, but equity protections prevent price from becoming the rationing mechanism for essential care.
5. **Rules-based digital claims platform:** comparable conceptually to MBS/HICAPS-like claiming, but designed for New Zealand.
6. **Top-tier accountability:** primary care and ambulance KPIs sit alongside hospital targets.

## What must be tested

- Whether dominant capitation suppresses marginal supply under workforce constraints.
- Whether PHO intermediation adds more value than transaction cost.
- Whether ACC fee-for-service currently stabilises primary care supply.
- Whether ambulance treat-and-refer/hear-and-treat pathways reduce ED and admission pressure.
- Whether co-payment settings can balance consumer choice, fiscal sustainability and equity.
- Whether a broader provider base can safely expand primary care supply.

## Key counterarguments

- Demand-driven funding may inflate activity and cost.
- Co-payments may worsen inequity.
- Scope-based expansion may fragment care.
- PHOs may provide valuable population-health functions that are not captured by a claims model.
- Hospitals may still grow because of ageing, multimorbidity and technology even if primary care expands.

## Response

The proposed model does not abandon capitation, clinical governance or population health. It separates **population-health funding** from **contact-type benefits**. The central modelling task is to test whether transaction-level constraints can achieve better access and fiscal control than fixed-envelope upstream rationing.

## Evidence anchors

- NZ Ministry of Health states capitation is the core way general practice is funded and that reweighting is proposed from 1 July 2026.
- The Sapere briefing states the reweighting work has not considered fundamental changes to the overall primary care funding model.
- The Health and Disability System Review recommended that Tier 1 funding be ring-fenced and that PHO contracting no longer be mandatory, while moving toward direct payments to providers through new commissioning arrangements.
- Health NZ reports ambulance funding and performance through separate contracts and reports, suggesting ambulance is already a distinct accountability domain but not necessarily a top-tier system target.
