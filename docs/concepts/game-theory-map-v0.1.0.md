# Game-theory map

## Purpose

This document maps the repeated games embedded in primary care, ambulance and hospital funding architecture in New Zealand and Australia.

## Core game: the upstream constraint / hospital growth game

### Players

1. Ministers / Cabinet.
2. Treasury.
3. Ministry of Health.
4. Health NZ hospital operations.
5. Health NZ primary/community commissioning.
6. PHOs/locality intermediaries.
7. General practices and wider primary care providers.
8. Ambulance providers.
9. ACC.
10. Consumers/patients.
11. Opposition parties/media/public accountability system.

### Strategies

Government/funder strategies:

- **Tight upstream control:** fixed budgets, capitation, PHO mediation, limited activity-sensitive payment.
- **Open contact benefits:** demand-driven benefits for defined contact types, broad provider eligibility and co-payment settings.
- **Hospital rescue:** prioritise hospital deficits, ED pressure, planned care and acute capacity.
- **Upstream expansion:** invest in marginal primary/urgent/ambulance capacity.

Provider strategies:

- **Expand supply:** open books, add staff, use multidisciplinary teams, add urgent slots.
- **Ration supply:** close books, increase co-payments, shorten visits, shift to telehealth, limit complex patients.
- **Substitute supply:** pharmacists, nurse practitioners, paramedics and allied health generate appropriate contacts.

Consumer strategies:

- **Use primary care early.**
- **Delay care.**
- **Use telehealth.**
- **Use ED/ambulance as default access.**

### Status quo equilibrium hypothesis

If upstream sectors are tightly controlled and hospitals are politically unavoidable, rational decision-makers repeatedly fund hospitals after demand becomes acute. Practices respond to weak marginal funding by rationing supply. Patients delay, pay more or use ED/ambulance. The hospital system grows, reinforcing the initial budget pressure.

### Alternative equilibrium hypothesis

If contact-type benefits make clinically appropriate upstream supply expandable, providers have a marginal incentive to deliver care earlier. Consumers can choose earlier care with co-payment signals and equity protections. Hospitals receive fewer avoidable presentations, though total primary care activity and public subsidy may rise.

## Payoff sketch

| Funder architecture | Provider response | Expected system outcome | Payoff interpretation |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tight upstream control | Ration supply | Hospital growth, patient delay, practice stress | Negative-sum over time |
| Tight upstream control | Expand supply | Provider financial stress and exit risk | Unstable |
| Open contact benefits | Ration supply | Higher fees, unmet need persists | Failure of supply response |
| Open contact benefits | Expand supply | Better access, possible hospital avoidance, fiscal risk | Potential positive-sum with safeguards |

## PHO intermediation game

PHOs can create value if they reduce coordination costs, improve equity, support quality improvement and deliver population-health functions. They create cost if they increase contracting friction, obscure payment flows, slow market entry or act as gatekeepers to funding.

The analytical distinction:

> PHO functions are not the same thing as PHO intermediation.

## ACC spillover game

ACC's activity-sensitive funding may support practice viability and marginal supply. If ACC constrains fee-for-service in isolation, it may improve ACC's immediate fiscal position while weakening whole-practice viability, thereby reducing non-injury primary care supply and shifting demand elsewhere.

This is a cross-funder externality problem.

## Ambulance game

If ambulance funding rewards conveyance or is constrained around response rather than resolution, patients may flow to ED even when safe alternatives exist. If treat-and-refer, hear-and-treat and alternative destination pathways are funded and measured, ambulance can become hospital-avoidance infrastructure.

## Australian game

Australia differs because Commonwealth funding of primary care and state/territory responsibility for public hospitals splits costs and benefits. Commonwealth investment in primary care may create state hospital savings; states may fund hospital rescue because they bear operational pressure. This creates underinvestment risk in primary care even where MBS supports marginal supply.

## Modelable variables

- hospital salience coefficient;
- upstream marginal payment coefficient;
- co-payment elasticity;
- provider entry threshold;
- provider exit/burnout threshold;
- PHO transaction-cost coefficient;
- ACC activity revenue share;
- ambulance non-conveyance payment rate;
- political penalty for ED/hospital failure;
- political penalty for co-payment growth;
- equity penalty for unmet need.
