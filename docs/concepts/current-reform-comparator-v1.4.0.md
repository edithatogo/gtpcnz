# Current reform comparator v1.4.0

## Why the comparator changed

The current policy baseline is not simply “capitation reweighting”. The public record shows a broader reform pathway.

## Components of the current reform pathway

1. **Capitation reweighting** from 1 July 2026, subject to sector negotiation, adding age, sex, multimorbidity, rurality and deprivation variables.
2. **Primary care access target**, proposed as more than 80 percent of people accessing an appointment with a general practice provider within one week.
3. **National Primary Care Dataset**, collecting appointment and encounter information from participating practices from April 2026 and supporting target reporting from July 2026.
4. **Performance-based funding**, including immunisation-linked payments and outcome frameworks.
5. **Digital 24/7 GP care**, intended to expand access where local general practice is not immediately available.
6. **Urgent and after-hours expansion**, including Budget 2025 investment and a 98 percent one-hour urgent-care access aim.
7. **Workforce initiatives**, including general practitioner and nurse practitioner training/placement initiatives.
8. **PHO accountability work**, including advice on PHO remit/function and stronger outcome/performance measures.
9. **Primary/community appropriation structure**, which formally separates primary/community/public/population services from hospital/specialist services.

## Updated evaluation question

The question is not whether current reform exists. It does.

The question is:

> Does the current reform pathway change the strategic game enough to allow safe upstream supply to expand, while preserving equity, place accountability and fiscal control?

## Scenario implications

Future model and MCDA scenarios should compare:

- Current reform pathway;
- Current reform + place accountability;
- Current reform + uncapped eligible primary medical fee-for-service;
- Current reform + uncapped eligible primary medical fee-for-service + place accountability;
- Current reform + urgent/ambulance alternatives;
- Current reform + provider-scope expansion;
- Weak-control demand-led model;
- Hospital investment priority.
