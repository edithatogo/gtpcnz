# Public thesis v1.7.2

## One-sentence version
New Zealand's current primary care reforms may be necessary, but the unresolved question is whether they change the supply game enough to let lower-cost upstream care expand before unmet need becomes hospital demand.

## The preferred architecture

1. **Capitation** for continuity, enrolment, proactive care, baseline viability and population responsibility.
2. **Uncapped scheduled fee-for-service for eligible primary medical activity**, controlled at the item, scope, audit, documentation, clinical-governance and accountability level.
3. **Place-based accountability** so providers cannot simply cherry-pick easy contacts and leave complex, rural, Māori, Pacific, disabled or high-deprivation populations behind.
4. **Co-payment protections** so price does not become the mechanism of rationing for high-need patients.
5. **Scope-enabled supply** across general practitioners, nurse practitioners, nurses, pharmacists, paramedics, allied health and other providers where the service is within scope and governance.
6. **Urgent care, ambulance and Accident Compensation Corporation interface design** so the upstream system can safely avoid avoidable emergency department flow.
7. **Data, audit and top-tier accountability** to detect gaming, low-value activity, inequity, fragmentation and unmet need.

## What this is not

- Not a claim that capitation is bad.
- Not an argument for uncontrolled fee-for-service.
- Not a proposal to abolish Primary Health Organisations.
- Not a claim that current reforms are trivial.
- Not a calibrated prediction of hospital savings.
- Not a RACMA-sponsored or RACMA-led project.

## Most cautious public phrasing

> Current reforms are stronger than a straw man. The issue is whether capitation reweighting, targets, data, digital access, urgent-care investment and PHO accountability remove the marginal supply constraint. If they do not, a scheduled, rules-based activity stream may be needed so eligible primary medical care can expand safely.

## What would weaken the thesis

The thesis should be softened if the current reform pathway materially improves appointment access, reduces closed books, lowers co-payment-related unmet need, strengthens rural in-person care and reduces avoidable emergency department/ambulance pressure without a new activity-sensitive payment stream.
