# Document incorporation report v1.4.0

## Bottom line

The email trail does not overturn the core thesis. It changes its precision.

The updated thesis is no longer that primary care is simply a residual inside a hospital-dominated Health New Zealand budget. Public documents show separate appropriations for hospital/specialist services and primary/community/public/population health services. The sharper thesis is that separate appropriations reduce direct diversion risk but do not remove the strategic game: visible hospital demand, baseline pressure, target salience, urgent-care policy, capitation design, PHO pass-through, ACC/Health New Zealand cross-funder rules and provider scope all influence where demand is allowed to grow.

## Major updates incorporated

### 1. Current reform is now treated as the comparator
The package now treats the current reform pathway as a real and substantial comparator: capitation reweighting, primary care access target, National Primary Care Dataset, performance-based funding, digital 24/7 care, urgent/after-hours expansion, workforce initiatives and PHO accountability work.

The question is no longer “why is New Zealand doing only capitation?”. The question is “does the current reform change the game enough?”.

### 2. Budget architecture wording corrected
Earlier language implying primary care funding was purely residual inside the Health NZ hospital budget has been replaced. The current wording recognises formal separate appropriations, while still analysing operational and political salience.

### 3. PHO critique narrowed
The PHO analysis now distinguishes:

- PHO/locality functions that may add value: place-based planning, population health, quality improvement, outreach, data support and integration.
- PHO intermediation risks: pass-through uncertainty, financial opacity, transaction cost, market-entry friction and strategic positioning by corporate or provider-owned entities.

### 4. Place-based accountability added as a guardrail
The benefits-schedule proposal now requires place-based population accountability. This addresses the central critique that a demand-led fee-for-service stream could otherwise incentivise cherry-picking of easy, profitable work.

### 5. Urgent and after-hours care added as a central policy shock
The $164m Budget 2025 urgent/after-hours programme and 98% one-hour access aim have been moved into the core comparator and game map. Urgent care is no longer peripheral; it is a key hospital-deflection setting.

### 6. ACC language corrected
“CDP” language has been removed from the ACC argument. The ACC analogy now refers to Cost of Treatment Regulations, contracts, purchase orders, PHAS, regulated item contributions, co-payments, scope, documentation, pre-approval and commissioning.

### 7. Formula-fixation precedent added
The Population-Based Funding Formula literature is added as a cautionary precedent. Formula debates can become prolonged and politically unstable, because stakeholders argue about allocative fairness while deeper supply and accountability games remain unresolved.

### 8. Substack track expanded
The Substack track now includes an expanded 18-post series with microeconomic diagrams, game-by-game explainers, a hybrid-game explanation, composite modelling summary, MCDA decision-support post and recommendations.

## Updated one-line thesis

New Zealand has begun a real primary care reform pathway, but the key question is whether it changes the game enough: eligible primary medical activity should become uncapped at the activity level and partly fee-for-service, controlled by ACC-like rules, while capitation and place-based accountability preserve continuity, population responsibility and equity.
