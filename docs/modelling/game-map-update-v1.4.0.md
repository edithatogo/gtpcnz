# Game-map update v1.4.0

The v1.4.0 update adds documents from the email trail and updates the mapped games.

## New and revised games

### G15 — Place-based accountability / cherry-picking game

- **Poor equilibrium:** providers expand easy, profitable, low-complexity contacts while complex, rural, Māori, Pacific, disabled, older and hard-to-reach populations remain under-served.
- **Better equilibrium:** national benefits are combined with place-based responsibility, outreach obligations, equity metrics and comprehensive population accountability.

### G16 — Current reform sufficiency game

- **Poor equilibrium:** current reform improves measurement, weighting and access targets but does not materially alter marginal supply or provider behaviour.
- **Better equilibrium:** current reform becomes the base on which uncapped eligible activity, urgent-care alternatives, direct claiming and place accountability are layered.

### G17 — Formula-fixation game

- **Poor equilibrium:** stakeholders contest weights and allocative shares indefinitely, while the supply architecture remains capped.
- **Better equilibrium:** formula improvements continue, but the main reform conversation shifts to marginal supply, patient flow, access, accountability and system outcomes.

### G18 — Urgent-care policy game

- **Poor equilibrium:** urgent care is funded as a separate access promise but not integrated with primary care, ambulance and hospital-flow incentives.
- **Better equilibrium:** urgent and after-hours services become an explicit hospital-deflection layer with shared data, shared accountability and scope-enabled staffing.

### G19 — Uncapped entitlement / fiscal-governance game

- **Poor equilibrium:** hard global caps suppress marginal primary medical supply; or, conversely, loose fee-for-service creates gaming and fiscal leakage.
- **Better equilibrium:** eligible activity is uncapped at the envelope level but constrained by scheduled prices, scope, clinical necessity, documentation, audit, co-payment protections and place-based obligations.

## Updated synthesis

The final policy problem is not a binary contest between capitation and fee-for-service. It is a hybrid game:

1. capitation distributes baseline responsibility;
2. fee-for-service restores the marginal supply signal for eligible medical activity;
3. place accountability prevents cherry-picking;
4. urgent/ambulance integration reduces hospital flow;
5. data and KPIs make upstream performance visible;
6. co-payment protections prevent price becoming the rationing mechanism;
7. audit and item rules prevent uncontrolled volume.
