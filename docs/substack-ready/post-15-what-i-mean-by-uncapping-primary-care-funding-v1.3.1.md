# Post 15 — What I mean by uncapping primary care funding

When I say primary care funding should be uncapped, I do not mean a blank cheque.

I mean something more specific.

At the moment, a lot of New Zealand general practice funding is built around capitation. Capitation means a practice gets a fixed amount of public funding each year for each enrolled person. That payment can be weighted, so some patients bring more funding than others. A person with more complex needs should attract more funding than a healthy young adult.

That makes sense for some things.

Capitation is good for continuity. It helps support enrolled populations. It can fund proactive care, care coordination and baseline team viability. It can also be used to give more money to practices serving people with higher needs.

But capitation has a problem.

Once a patient is enrolled, the next appointment is work. It takes clinician time, room time, documentation, risk, follow-up, prescriptions, referrals and administration. Under a strongly capped model, the extra public funding for that extra appointment may be weak or absent.

So the system rations.

It rations through waiting. It rations through closed books. It rations through short appointments. It rations through higher co-payments. And sometimes it rations by letting the problem become someone else's problem: urgent care, ambulance, the emergency department, or hospital.

That is why I think part of primary care needs to move closer to an Accident Compensation Corporation style model.

The Accident Compensation Corporation does not simply hand out unlimited money. It sets rules. It says who can be paid, what services can be paid for, when treatment is clinically necessary, whether the provider is qualified, and how much the public contribution will be.

That is the kind of logic I mean.

For ordinary primary care, New Zealand could create a Primary Care Benefits Schedule. Eligible medical primary care contacts would attract a scheduled public contribution when the service is actually delivered. The total number of eligible contacts would not be fixed by a hard annual cap.

The item price would be controlled. The rules would be controlled. The provider scope would be controlled. The billing would be audited. But the total eligible activity would be allowed to grow when people need care and providers can safely deliver it.

That distinction matters.

A capped pool says:

> Here is the amount of money. Work out how to ration it.

An uncapped benefit says:

> Here is the type of care the public system will contribute to. If eligible care is delivered under the rules, the payment flows.

That would create a marginal supply signal. It would make the next clinically necessary appointment more viable.

But there is a danger.

If we simply uncap activity with weak rules, we may get the wrong kind of growth. Easy work may expand. Simple contacts may multiply. Providers may avoid complex, rural or hard-to-reach patients. Costs may grow without improving health. That is why uncapping must be paired with place-based accountability.

The best model is not just fee-for-service.

It is:

> capitation for continuity, fee-for-service for eligible medical activity, and place-based responsibility so the system still has to look after the whole community.

That means co-payment protections for people who cannot afford care. It means rural safeguards. It means Māori and Pacific health obligations. It means clinical governance. It means data. It means audit. It means measuring whether care is actually preventing emergency department and hospital pressure.

So no, I am not arguing for an uncontrolled market.

I am arguing that New Zealand should stop pretending a capped primary care envelope can meet uncapped human need.

The question is not whether primary care should have rules.

It should.

The question is whether the main rule should be a hard cap that suppresses supply, or a clinical schedule that lets safe care expand.
