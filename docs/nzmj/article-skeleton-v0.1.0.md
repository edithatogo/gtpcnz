## Introduction

Primary care funding debates often focus on the quantum of funding. Funding quantum matters, but architecture also matters. Payment rules shape provider behaviour, consumer choices, market entry, professional substitution and whether unmet need is absorbed upstream or appears later in emergency departments and hospitals.

Aotearoa New Zealand and Australia provide useful contrasting cases. New Zealand relies heavily on capitation and PHO-mediated arrangements, while Australia relies more heavily on Medicare fee-for-service with increasing interest in blended payment. Both systems face hospital pressure, rural workforce constraints, chronic disease burden and access concerns.

This paper asks whether current policy may be missing the whole-system game: tightly controlling lower-cost upstream activity may unintentionally channel growth into higher-cost hospital care.

## Discussion

The central policy question is not whether capitation or fee-for-service is inherently superior. It is which rules create the desired equilibrium. Capitation can support continuity and population health, but a dominant capitation model may under-reward marginal activity. Fee-for-service supports marginal supply, but may create low-value volume. A blended architecture should use each payment signal deliberately.

A demand-driven Primary Care Benefits Schedule would move the system away from fixed-envelope rationing and toward transaction-level control. Eligible contact types, provider scope, clinical governance, co-payment settings and equity protections would constrain use while allowing supply to expand.
