# Primary care funding as a repeated game: why New Zealand should look beyond capitation reweighting

## Abstract

### Aim

To argue that New Zealand's primary care reform should look beyond capitation reweighting and consider the whole-system incentives that shape primary, urgent, ambulance/prehospital and hospital demand.

### Methods

This Viewpoint uses a policy architecture and repeated-game framing to examine how tightly managed upstream funding may shift unmet need into higher-cost hospital care.

### Results

Capitation reweighting may improve allocation within primary care, but it does not necessarily remove marginal supply constraints. A repeated-game framing suggests that hospital pressure is more visible and politically salient than dispersed primary care access failure, creating a risk that the system funds hospital rescue rather than upstream supply.

### Conclusions

New Zealand should test a National Primary Care Benefits Schedule alongside capitation. Eligible contact types should be funded through a rules-based, demand-responsive architecture available to accredited providers acting within scope, with co-payment protections, clinical governance, audit and hospital-equivalent accountability for primary care and ambulance outcomes.

## Introduction

New Zealand's primary care debate is often framed as a contest between capitation and fee-for-service. That framing is too narrow. The more important question is whether current funding architecture creates the equilibrium the health system wants.

A model that tightly controls lower-cost primary, urgent and ambulance/prehospital activity may unintentionally channel demand growth into higher-cost hospital care. Capitation reweighting is necessary, but it may miss the wider system game.

## Primary care funding is a game, not just a formula

Primary care funding debates often begin with a formula. Which patients should attract more funding? Which variables should be weighted? How should age, sex, rurality, deprivation, ethnicity and multimorbidity be treated? These are important questions. New Zealand's current capitation formula was introduced in the early 2000s, and current reform work is intended to improve the distribution of funding to better reflect need.

But a formula is only one part of the game. Funding architecture also determines who can generate activity, how easily providers can enter the market, how much marginal work is funded, what consumers pay, how unmet need becomes visible, and which part of the system receives political attention when demand grows.

New Zealand may therefore be missing the wood for the trees. Capitation reweighting can improve distribution within primary care. It does not necessarily test whether primary care funding architecture itself constrains supply.

## The problem with capitation is not that capitation is wrong

Capitation has important advantages. It supports enrolled-population accountability, continuity, proactive care and budget predictability. It can be weighted for equity and need. It also supports team-based care that does not always fit neatly into fee-for-service billing.

The problem arises when capitation becomes too dominant as the access mechanism. Once a patient is enrolled, additional clinically necessary care has positive marginal cost: clinician time, nursing time, room use, administration, documentation, follow-up, risk and opportunity cost. In a strongly capitated system, marginal public revenue for the next contact may be weak or absent. Unless offset by co-payments, ACC payments, programme funding or other activity-sensitive payments, the rational supply response may be shorter appointments, waiting time, closed books, higher charges, triage or substitution to lower-cost models.

The policy conclusion is not “fee-for-service good, capitation bad.” It is that different types of care require different payment signals.

## Reweighting may solve allocation, not supply

The current capitation reweighting work is valuable because it improves estimation of relative cost and need. However, the released briefing describes the work as a technical analysis to improve the capitation formula and states that it has not considered fundamental changes to the overall primary care funding model.

That limitation matters. An allocation model asks: how should funding be distributed across enrolled populations? A supply model asks: what happens when patients want care, providers can deliver it, but the funding architecture does not allow marginal activity to grow? A system model asks a third question: where does unmet need go?

The concern is that unmet need does not disappear. It may reappear as emergency department demand, ambulance escalation, hospital admission, delayed diagnosis, avoidable deterioration or higher patient charges.

## A repeated game favours hospital rescue

A simplified repeated game helps explain the risk.

The players are government/funder, upstream providers, hospitals and consumers. In each period, the funder chooses whether to tightly control upstream funding or allow defined upstream activity to expand. Providers choose whether to expand or ration supply. Consumers choose early care, delayed care, telehealth, ambulance or emergency department attendance. Hospital pressure then materialises. The funder responds, and the next period begins.

In this game, hospital pressure has high political salience. Hospitals are visible, acute, publicly owned, directly managed and difficult to ignore. Primary care access problems are dispersed. They may appear as closed books, waiting times, rising co-payments, workforce stress or non-attendance. These signals are real but less concentrated.

This can create a negative-sum equilibrium. The system controls upstream costs, unmet need flows downstream, hospital pressure grows, and further resources are directed to hospitals because the crisis is visible and unavoidable. No actor needs to behave irrationally for this to happen. It is a product of the rules.

## A benefits schedule would control transactions, not suppress activity

New Zealand should test a different architecture: a National Primary Care Benefits Schedule.

This would not remove capitation. Capitation would remain for continuity, population accountability, proactive care, care coordination and equity-adjusted baseline funding. But access-related marginal activity would be funded through a nationally administered benefit stream for defined contact types.

The benefit schedule would pay public subsidies for eligible primary, urgent and ambulance/prehospital contacts delivered by accredited providers acting within scope. It would be constrained by contact definitions, provider eligibility, clinical governance, co-payment rules, data reporting and audit. It would not be an uncontrolled blank cheque. It would be demand-driven within rules.

Eligible contact types might include urgent same-day primary care, rural in-person assessment, complex multimorbidity review, pharmacist-led medicines optimisation, nurse practitioner acute and chronic care, physiotherapist first-contact musculoskeletal assessment, primary mental health contacts, wound care, minor procedures, extended-care paramedic assessment, hear-and-treat, treat-and-refer and safe non-conveyance with follow-up.

This moves the system from blunt envelope control to transaction-level control.

## Funding should follow contact types, not guild boundaries

A benefits schedule also enables workforce reform. Primary care should not be defined only by a traditional general practice staffing ratio. It should be defined by clinically appropriate contacts, delivered by providers whose scope, competence and governance allow them to perform the work safely.

General practitioners remain essential, particularly for diagnostic complexity, undifferentiated illness, continuity and clinical governance. But many contacts can be delivered by nurse practitioners, pharmacists, physiotherapists, nurses, mental health practitioners, paramedics, kaupapa Maori providers, Pacific providers and community teams.

This is not de-professionalisation. It is scope-based supply. Prescribing regulation, scopes of practice, credentialing, escalation rules and clinical governance determine what each provider can do. The funding model should not add an unnecessary professional bottleneck on top of those safety rules.

## Co-payment can signal demand, but must not become the equity failure

A demand-driven benefit stream raises a legitimate concern: utilisation may grow. Some growth would be desirable if it reflects previously unmet need. Some may be low value. Co-payments can provide a demand signal, but they can also ration care by income.

The answer is calibrated co-payment architecture. Priority groups and essential contacts should have zero or low co-payments. Co-payments should be capped or prohibited for some contact types. Benefits should be higher for deprivation, rurality, disability, Maori and Pacific peoples, and multimorbidity. Fee transparency and monitoring of unmet need due to cost should be mandatory.

The aim is not user-pays primary care. The aim is to let consumers choose eligible lower-cost care while preventing price from becoming the mechanism by which high-needs patients are excluded.

## PHOs: separate function from intermediation

PHOs may add value through population health, quality improvement, data support, locality relationships and equity work. But those functions are not the same as being the gateway to public payment.

The better question is: which PHO functions should be funded because they add value, and which payment-gateway functions can be replaced by a national rules-based claims platform?

## Ambulance is part of the same upstream architecture

Ambulance should not be treated only as transport to hospital. In the right funding and clinical governance architecture, ambulance and prehospital care can be part of hospital avoidance. Hear-and-treat, treat-and-refer, extended-care paramedics and alternative destination pathways can resolve some demand without ED conveyance.

Ambulance data and accountability structures should be lifted into top-tier system performance alongside hospitals and primary care.

## Accountability should be hospital-equivalent

Targets shape attention. New Zealand has highly visible hospital targets. It is now developing a primary care access target and National Primary Care Dataset. This is welcome, but the accountability architecture should go further.

Top-tier indicators should include primary care access, same-day urgent care access, enrolment and open-book access, co-payment burden, continuity, rural in-person capacity, ambulatory-sensitive hospitalisations, ambulance response times, ambulance non-conveyance with safe follow-up, and ambulance-hospital handover delay. All should be stratified by ethnicity, deprivation, disability and rurality.

Primary care and ambulance should not merely be inputs to hospital performance. They should be visible system performance domains in their own right.

## A research agenda

This proposal should be tested empirically. System dynamics modelling could represent feedback loops between primary care access, unmet need, ED demand, hospital pressure and budget response. Agent-based modelling could represent heterogeneous patients, providers, PHOs, telehealth providers and rural communities. A formal repeated-game model could identify conditions under which hospital-rescue equilibria dominate upstream investment.

The key scenarios are status quo, capitation reweighting only, reweighting plus access targets, reweighting plus an activity-sensitive benefit stream, reduced PHO intermediation, rural loading, ambulance treat-and-refer expansion, and ACC fee-for-service constraint.

The falsifiable question is simple: does expanding eligible lower-cost primary and prehospital activity reduce avoidable growth in hospital demand without worsening equity, fragmentation or low-value use?

## Conclusion

Capitation reweighting is necessary but not sufficient. It may improve fairness within primary care, but it does not answer whether the funding architecture suppresses upstream supply and channels growth into hospitals. New Zealand should test a demand-driven, rules-based Primary Care Benefits Schedule alongside capitation, broader provider eligibility, calibrated co-payment protections and hospital-equivalent accountability for primary care and ambulance outcomes.

The future should not be capitation versus fee-for-service. It should be a deliberately designed game in which the rational equilibrium is timely, equitable, lower-cost care before hospital pressure becomes unavoidable.
