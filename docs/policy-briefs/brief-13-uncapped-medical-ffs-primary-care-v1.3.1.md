# Policy brief 13 — Remove the cap: an ACC-style FFS stream for primary medical care — v1.3.1

## Proposal

New Zealand should retain capitation, but add an **uncapped, demand-led fee-for-service stream for eligible primary medical care**.

This is not a capped programme. It is a scheduled public contribution that flows when eligible care is delivered.

## Why this is needed

Capitation is useful for continuity, enrolment and population accountability. But if it remains the dominant access payment, it can weaken the marginal incentive to provide additional clinically necessary care. Once a patient is enrolled, another appointment creates work, risk and cost, but often little additional public funding.

A fixed envelope may look fiscally tidy, but patient need does not disappear. It can reappear as waiting, co-payments, closed books, urgent care pressure, ambulance demand, emergency department demand and hospital admissions.

## What should be uncapped?

Eligible primary medical contacts, for example:

- acute primary care consultations;
- urgent assessment in primary care;
- complex consultations;
- procedures that can safely occur in primary care;
- follow-up that prevents escalation;
- in-person rural contacts;
- some nurse practitioner, nurse and team-based contacts where clinically equivalent and within scope.

## What should remain controlled?

- Item values.
- Eligible contact definitions.
- Provider credentialing and scope.
- Clinical necessity.
- Duration and frequency rules.
- Documentation.
- Prior approval for selected services.
- Audit and unusual billing analytics.
- Co-payment protections.
- Place-based population accountability.

## The ACC analogy

ACC already shows that New Zealand can operate an activity-sensitive treatment payment system. Under ACC, payment depends on regulations, contracts or agreements, and treatment must be clinically necessary, appropriate, within scope and documented.

A primary care equivalent should adapt that logic for ordinary health need.

## Safeguards

The model must not become “uncapped easy work”. It needs:

1. place-based accountability;
2. high-need population obligations;
3. rural/in-person safeguards;
4. co-payment protections;
5. anti-gaming analytics;
6. transparent public reporting;
7. clinical governance by scope and competence;
8. integration with urgent care, ambulance and hospital pathways.

## Core line

> **Remove the global cap on eligible primary medical activity. Keep prices, scope, clinical rules, audit and equity safeguards.**
