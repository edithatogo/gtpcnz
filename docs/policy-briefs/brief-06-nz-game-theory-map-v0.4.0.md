# Policy brief 06: The New Zealand primary care and ambulance funding game

## Summary

New Zealand is not simply underfunding primary care. It may be running a repeated policy game in which lower-cost upstream sectors are tightly managed, while growth is allowed to appear in the higher-cost hospital system where pressure is visible, urgent and politically unavoidable.

The issue is not capitation alone, PHOs alone, ambulance alone, or hospital deficits alone. The issue is how those subsystems interact.

## Core claim

Current reform is focused on improving capitation weights and measuring primary care access. Those steps are useful, but they may not change the rules that determine whether more safe, lower-cost contacts can actually be generated.

A reweighted capitation formula may improve allocation across enrolled populations. It does not necessarily create more appointments, rural in-person services, urgent primary care capacity, pharmacist-led medicines contacts, nurse-practitioner contacts, allied-health contacts, paramedic treat-and-refer pathways or market entry by new providers.

The larger question is:

> What rules of the game would allow safe, lower-cost, upstream care to expand before unmet need becomes hospital demand?

## The dominant repeated game

1. Primary care, urgent care and ambulance/prehospital care are managed through fixed or quasi-fixed budgets, capitation, contracts, PHOs, professional constraints and tight programme rules.
2. Providers respond rationally by rationing access, closing books, raising co-payments, narrowing service models or not entering the market.
3. Patients respond rationally by delaying care, seeking telehealth for some needs, calling ambulance or attending ED.
4. Hospitals become the visible pressure valve.
5. Health NZ and Ministers respond to the visible hospital problem.
6. The upstream constraint remains, and the game repeats.

This is **hospital-rescue dominance**.

## The mapped games

| Game | Core policy concern |
|---|---|
| Hospital-salience budget game | Hospital pressure is visible and politically costly; upstream failure is dispersed. |
| Health NZ internal allocation game | Health NZ operates hospitals and commissions primary/community care, creating asymmetric operational salience. |
| Capitation marginal-supply game | Capitation supports continuity but weakens the marginal signal for additional clinically necessary contacts. |
| Consumer access pathway game | Patients choose between early care, delay, telehealth, ambulance and ED based on cost, wait, trust and access. |
| PHO intermediation game | PHOs may add value, but mandatory payment intermediation may add friction, opacity and entry barriers. |
| ACC/Health NZ cross-funder game | ACC activity funding may stabilise general primary care supply; siloed ACC constraint may shift costs. |
| Ambulance conveyance game | Conveyance can become the default if alternatives are not funded, governed or indemnified. |
| Scope-of-practice supply game | Funding eligibility may be narrower than legal or clinical scope. |
| Telehealth/local-supply game | Telehealth can extend access but may erode local in-person supply if not integrated. |
| Co-payment calibration game | Co-payments can signal demand but can also worsen inequity and delayed care. |
| KPI salience game | What is measured at top-tier level gets managed; narrow targets can be gamed. |
| Equity and trust game | Average access can improve while equity worsens unless designed into the mechanism. |
| Political economy game | Reform can be framed as pro-market, anti-PHO, anti-GP or anti-equity unless positioned carefully. |
| Data observability game | Unmeasured upstream failure remains less fundable than measured hospital pressure. |

## Why capitation reweighting is necessary but insufficient

Capitation should remain part of the model. It supports continuity, enrolment, population responsibility and baseline viability. Reweighting for need is sensible.

But capitation reweighting mainly answers an allocation question:

> How should existing primary care funding be distributed more fairly across enrolled populations?

It does not fully answer the supply question:

> How should the system allow clinically appropriate primary, urgent and ambulance care to expand when patients need it?

## Policy mechanism: a Primary Care Benefits Schedule

The reform option is not uncontrolled fee-for-service. It is a nationally defined, rules-based benefit schedule for eligible contact types.

Core features:

- retain capitation for continuity and enrolled-population responsibility;
- define eligible primary, urgent and ambulance/prehospital contact types;
- allow accredited providers to claim within legal scope, credentialing and clinical governance;
- use co-payments as a calibrated demand signal, with concessions, caps and equity protections;
- separate PHO/locality functions from mandatory payment-gateway functions;
- include ambulance alternatives such as hear-and-treat, treat-and-refer, alternative destination and safe follow-up;
- audit low-value activity, fragmentation and gaming;
- evaluate against access, continuity, equity, patient charges, ambulance conveyance, ED demand and avoidable hospitalisation.

## Provider eligibility

Funding should follow clinically appropriate contact types, not professional guild boundaries.

Eligible activity could be generated by:

- GPs;
- nurse practitioners;
- nurses operating under appropriate governance;
- pharmacists and pharmacist prescribers;
- physiotherapists and other allied health providers;
- paramedics and extended-care paramedics;
- kaupapa Maori and Pacific providers;
- integrated urgent care and telehealth providers.

Scopes of practice, prescribing regulation, credentialing and clinical governance should determine what a provider can safely do. The funding model should not artificially restrict supply to one professional group.

## Top-tier accountability

Primary care and ambulance should not merely be inputs to hospital performance. They should be top-tier system performance domains.

A possible top-tier framework:

| Domain | Indicator |
|---|---|
| Primary care access | appointments accessed within seven days, stratified by region and equity group |
| Same-day urgent access | same-day urgent primary or after-hours access |
| Enrolment and market access | closed books, new-patient acceptance and non-enrolled access |
| Co-payment burden | median and high-percentile patient charges by group and region |
| Continuity | usual provider or usual team continuity |
| Ambulance response | time-critical response times by rurality |
| Ambulance alternatives | hear-and-treat, treat-and-refer and safe non-conveyance rates |
| Ambulance-hospital interface | ED handover delay and ambulance time lost |
| Hospital avoidance | ambulatory-sensitive and potentially preventable hospitalisations |
| Safety and quality | revisits, adverse events and patient experience |

## What should be modelled

Simulation modelling should compare:

1. status quo;
2. capitation reweighting only;
3. capitation reweighting plus access target;
4. benefits schedule for GP-only contacts;
5. benefits schedule with broader provider eligibility;
6. benefits schedule plus co-payment protections;
7. ambulance alternatives funded as eligible contacts;
8. PHO optionality/direct claiming;
9. ACC fee-for-service constrained in isolation;
10. top-tier primary and ambulance KPI accountability.

Outcomes should include supply, waiting time, closed books, fees, unmet need, rural in-person capacity, ambulance conveyance, ED demand, avoidable admissions, equity and total public expenditure.

## Bottom line

The current debate risks asking how to distribute constrained primary care funding more fairly. The larger question is whether the system should continue constraining lower-cost access sectors and then funding the hospital growth that follows.

The policy question is therefore not simply:

> How should capitation be reweighted?

It is:

> What rules of the game would allow safe, lower-cost, upstream care to expand before unmet need becomes hospital demand?
