# Policy brief 2: PHO functions versus PHO intermediation

## Problem

PHO debates are often framed as either defend PHOs or abolish PHOs. That misses the key distinction.

> PHO functions may still be valuable, but PHO intermediation may not need to remain the gateway to public primary care funding.

## Useful PHO or locality functions

- population-health planning;
- equity work;
- quality improvement;
- data support;
- clinical network support;
- local integration;
- Māori and Pacific provider support;
- workforce support;
- after-hours coordination.

## Potential intermediation problems

- transaction costs;
- limited transparency of payment flows;
- slow market entry;
- gatekeeping of public funding;
- inconsistent pass-through arrangements;
- unclear accountability;
- complexity for new provider models.

## Evidence anchor

The Health and Disability System Review recommended that it should no longer be mandatory for DHBs to contract PHOs for primary health care services and that Tier 1 funding should increasingly be paid directly to providers through new commissioning arrangements. Recent Ministry material also states that the Ministry lacks direct access to PHO financial and ownership information, constraining monitoring and regulation.

## Policy options

1. Retain PHOs unchanged.
2. Require greater transparency and pass-through rules.
3. Make PHO membership optional for claiming public benefits.
4. Split PHO population-health functions from payment intermediation.
5. Replace PHO gateway functions with a national rules-based platform.

## Preferred testable proposal

Pilot direct provider claiming for defined primary care benefits while preserving locality/population-health functions through separately funded PHO-like or locality entities.

## Key modelling question

Does reducing PHO intermediation lower transaction costs and increase market entry without reducing population-health performance?
