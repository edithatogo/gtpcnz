# The Australian Commonwealth-state primary care game

## Core proposition

Australia's problem is not the same as New Zealand's. Australia has a stronger activity-payment signal through Medicare, but it also has a structural split between Commonwealth-funded primary care and state/territory hospital pressure.

## The game

The Commonwealth funds much of primary care through Medicare and related incentives. States and territories operate and fund public hospitals, with Commonwealth contributions through national agreements. If stronger primary care prevents hospital use, the benefits may accrue partly to states, while costs sit with the Commonwealth. This creates a split-incentive problem.

## Policy issue

Primary care investment may be socially efficient but fiscally unattractive to the payer that bears the upfront cost. Conversely, hospital rescue is politically salient for states and territories because ED demand, elective waits, ambulance ramping and acute capacity are visible.

## Comparison with New Zealand

| Issue | Australia | New Zealand |
|---|---|---|
| Dominant primary care signal | Fee-for-service with blended incentives | Capitation plus programme funding and co-payments |
| Main distortion | Commonwealth-state split | Hospital-dominated budget architecture and PHO-mediated capitation |
| Supply risk | Volume, short consults, low-value activity, rebate inadequacy | Weak marginal supply signal, closed books, co-payments, entry barriers |
| Best direction | Better blending and shared-savings/accountability | Benefits schedule plus retained capitation and broader provider eligibility |

## Policy options

- Shared savings arrangements where primary care investment reduces avoidable hospital demand.
- Stronger national/state joint accountability for avoidable hospitalisations, lower-urgency ED and primary care access.
- Blended payments for complex care and multidisciplinary teams.
- Rural and remote loadings that reflect fixed costs and low supply elasticity.
- Data linkage between primary care investment and hospital demand.

## RACMA angle

RACMA can add value by framing this as a whole-system design problem: the payer who can prevent demand is not always the payer who experiences the downstream pressure.
