# Hospital-equivalent accountability for primary care and ambulance

## Core proposition

Primary care and ambulance should be visible system performance domains in their own right, not merely background inputs to hospital performance.

## Why this matters

Targets shape attention. Hospitals have visible targets, reporting cycles and political consequences. Primary care and ambulance access failures are often more dispersed. If the accountability framework gives hospitals stronger visibility, the repeated game favours hospital rescue over upstream reform.

## Proposed top-tier KPI domains

### Primary care access

- Percentage of appointments accessed within seven days of booking.
- Same-day urgent primary care availability.
- Practice open-book/enrolment availability.
- Median and 90th percentile wait time by region.

### Patient cost and equity

- Median and 90th percentile co-payment.
- Unmet need due to cost.
- Stratification by ethnicity, deprivation, rurality, disability, age and multimorbidity.

### Continuity and quality

- Continuity with usual provider or team.
- Repeat unplanned contacts.
- Follow-up after urgent/ambulance/ED contact.
- Patient-reported experience.

### Ambulance and prehospital care

- Response times for time-critical events.
- Hear-and-treat and treat-and-refer rates.
- Safe non-conveyance outcomes.
- Re-contact and ED presentation after non-conveyance.
- Ambulance handover delay.

### Hospital avoidance

- Ambulatory-sensitive hospitalisations.
- Lower-urgency ED presentations, interpreted cautiously.
- Avoidable readmission where linked to primary care gaps.

## Governance recommendation

Top-tier reporting should include primary care, ambulance and hospital measures in the same performance dashboard. This would make upstream constraint visible before it becomes hospital pressure.
