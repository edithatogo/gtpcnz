# Policy brief 4: The Australian Commonwealth-state primary care game

## Problem

Australia's primary care problem differs from New Zealand's. Australia retains a strong MBS fee-for-service architecture that supports marginal supply, but the Commonwealth-state split creates a different strategic problem.

## Core game

- The Commonwealth funds much of primary care through Medicare.
- States and territories operate public hospitals and bear much acute operational pressure.
- Primary care investment may reduce hospital demand, but benefits may accrue outside the payer's budget.
- States fund hospital rescue because hospital failure is visible and immediate.

## Current reform anchor

The Australian Review of General Practice Incentives recommends a simplified, opt-in blended payment architecture, including a baseline practice payment based on patient need, socioeconomic status and location. It also explicitly envisages broader multidisciplinary practice teams and a reduced relative reliance on fee-for-service.

## Comparative lesson

Australia shows why pure fee-for-service is not enough. New Zealand shows why dominant capitation may not be enough. Both countries need blended funding, but with different rules of engagement.

## Policy implication

Australia should continue moving toward blended primary care payments, but also needs cross-government incentives so Commonwealth primary care investment and state hospital benefits are jointly recognised.

## Key modelling question

What cross-funder payment or shared-savings architecture would induce optimal Commonwealth-state investment in upstream care?
