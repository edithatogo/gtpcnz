# Ambulance, primary care and hospital avoidance

## Core proposition

Ambulance and prehospital care should be treated as part of upstream access architecture, not only as transport to hospital.

## Why this matters

Ambulance demand sits between primary care and hospitals. Some demand is truly time-critical and must be escalated rapidly. Some is urgent but manageable in the community with the right clinical governance. Some reflects lack of primary care access, after-hours access, rural access or aged-care support.

If ambulance funding is tightly constrained and performance is judged mainly through response time and conveyance, the system may miss opportunities to resolve appropriate cases earlier and avoid ED attendance.

## Contact types to consider in a Primary Care Benefits Schedule

- Hear-and-treat.
- Treat-and-refer.
- Extended-care paramedic assessment.
- Alternative destination transport.
- Safe non-conveyance with documented follow-up.
- Paramedic-initiated urgent primary care referral.
- Rural urgent response integrated with local primary care.
- Aged-care facility assessment and referral.

## Governance requirements

- Clear inclusion and exclusion criteria.
- Escalation rules.
- Prescribing/medicines protocols.
- Shared documentation.
- Follow-up accountability.
- Audit of adverse events and re-contact.
- Outcome monitoring by deprivation, rurality, ethnicity and age.

## KPIs

- Response times for time-critical events.
- Hear-and-treat rates.
- Treat-and-refer rates.
- Safe non-conveyance outcomes.
- Re-contact within 24/48/72 hours.
- ED attendance after non-conveyance.
- Ambulance handover delay.
- Patient experience.
- Rural response and follow-up equity.

## Policy implication

A primary care funding architecture that ignores ambulance will miss a critical upstream lever. The schedule should include defined ambulance/prehospital benefit items where the contact is clinically governed and has potential to safely avoid ED conveyance.
