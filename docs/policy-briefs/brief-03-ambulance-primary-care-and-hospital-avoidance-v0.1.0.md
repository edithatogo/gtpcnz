# Policy brief 3: Ambulance, primary care and hospital avoidance

## Problem

Ambulance is often treated as an emergency response service adjacent to hospitals. It should also be analysed as part of the lower-cost access and hospital-avoidance architecture.

## Core argument

Primary care, urgent care and ambulance/prehospital services form the upstream access system. If they are tightly constrained, patients flow into ED and hospitals. If they are funded to resolve appropriate care earlier, they may reduce avoidable hospital demand.

## Relevant ambulance contact types

- hear-and-treat;
- treat-and-refer;
- non-conveyance with safe follow-up;
- alternative destination pathways;
- extended care paramedic assessment;
- rural PRIME-type response;
- urgent community referral into primary care benefits.

## Current accountability issue

Ambulance providers report contract KPIs and response-time performance. But ambulance performance is not generally treated as a top-tier health-system target on the same political plane as ED and elective care.

## Proposed reform

Add ambulance KPIs to top-tier system accountability:

- time-critical response performance by rurality;
- safe non-conveyance rate;
- treat-and-refer follow-up completion;
- ambulance offload delay/time lost at ED;
- 111 calls resolved without ED attendance;
- patient outcomes after non-conveyance.

## Key modelling question

What mix of primary care benefits and ambulance non-conveyance/treat-and-refer payments minimises unsafe delay and avoidable ED/hospital flow?
