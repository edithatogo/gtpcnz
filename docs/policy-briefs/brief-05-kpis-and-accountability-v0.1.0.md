# Policy brief 5: Lift primary care and ambulance accountability to hospital-equivalent level

## Problem

What the system measures at the top level shapes what receives political attention. If hospital targets dominate, the system will keep responding to hospital pressure after upstream failure becomes visible.

## Proposal

Add primary care and ambulance KPIs to the top tier of health-system accountability.

## Primary care KPI candidates

- percentage of people able to access clinically appropriate primary care within seven days;
- same-day urgent primary care access;
- practices open to enrolment;
- median and 90th percentile co-payment by region, deprivation and age;
- continuity with usual provider/team;
- access by Māori, Pacific peoples, deprivation, rurality, disability and multimorbidity;
- proportion of benefits delivered by non-GP providers within scope;
- avoidable ED presentations and ambulatory-sensitive hospitalisations.

## Ambulance KPI candidates

- response time for immediately life-threatening events;
- response time by rurality;
- ambulance offload delay/time lost at ED;
- safe non-conveyance;
- hear-and-treat resolution;
- treat-and-refer completion and recontact rate;
- patient outcomes following non-conveyance.

## Design principle

KPIs should not punish providers for structural under-capacity. They should reveal system performance and link to funding architecture.

## Key modelling question

Which KPI set best identifies upstream constraint before it manifests as hospital demand?
