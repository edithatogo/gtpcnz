# PHO functions versus PHO intermediation

## Core proposition

PHO functions and PHO payment intermediation should be evaluated separately. Some PHO or locality functions may add value, but PHO membership should not necessarily remain the gateway to public primary care payment.

## Why this matters

New Zealand's primary care funding architecture is not only a set of payment formulas. It is also an administrative market structure. If public funding flows through PHOs and contracting arrangements that are opaque, slow or difficult for new providers to navigate, the architecture may create transaction costs and barriers to market entry.

The question is not whether PHOs are good or bad. The question is which functions are valuable and which are merely intermediation.

## Functions that may add value

- Population health analytics.
- Equity initiatives and outreach.
- Quality improvement.
- Locality relationships.
- Data support.
- Practice support.
- Māori and Pacific health partnerships.
- Workforce and service development.
- Shared services for small practices.

## Functions that may create friction

- Mandatory gatekeeping of public payment.
- Contracting complexity.
- Delayed or opaque pass-through.
- Variable administrative charges.
- Inconsistent onboarding of new providers.
- Difficulty monitoring reserves, ownership structures and financial flows.
- Entrenched market structure.

## Policy options

### Option 1: Retain PHOs but strengthen transparency

Require standard financial reporting, pass-through reporting, administrative-cost reporting and provider onboarding metrics.

### Option 2: Separate PHO functions from payment processing

Fund PHO/locality entities for population-health functions but move eligible contact payments to a national rules-based platform.

### Option 3: Optionalise PHO membership

Allow providers to access public benefits directly if they meet accreditation, reporting and governance requirements.

### Option 4: Replace PHO payment intermediation

Create a national primary care payment platform. Commission population-health and equity functions separately through contestable or locality-based arrangements.

## Evaluation questions

- What proportion of public primary care funding is retained, transformed or passed through by PHOs?
- What is the administrative cost of PHO intermediation?
- Does PHO membership improve equity and quality outcomes compared with direct payment plus separate locality commissioning?
- Does PHO intermediation affect new practice entry, rural services, telehealth entrants, Māori/Pacific providers, pharmacists, nurse practitioners or allied health providers?
- Can a national claims platform reduce transaction costs while preserving population-health functions?

## Recommended position

Do not begin with PHO abolition. Begin with function separation:

> Fund PHO functions where they add value. Do not require PHO intermediation where a national rules-based payment platform can pay accredited providers directly.
