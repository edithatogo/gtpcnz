# Policy brief 07 v0.7.0: What the modelling needs to show

## Summary

The game-theoretic mapping and demonstrative modelling now identify a plausible policy mechanism: tightly constrained primary care, urgent care and ambulance funding may shift unmet need into hospitals, where demand becomes visible, unavoidable and fundable.

The next policy question is not whether the model is persuasive conceptually. It is what evidence would be required before government, RACMA or sector partners should rely on it.

## Current status

The work has passed the conceptual and demonstrative stage:

- fourteen component games have been mapped;
- each game has an executable demonstrative model;
- five policy scenarios have been tested;
- uncertainty analysis has identified the most influential assumptions;
- audit artefacts distinguish public facts, theory, assumptions and policy options.

It has **not** yet passed empirical calibration.

## What the v0.7.0 uncertainty layer adds

The uncertainty analysis perturbs every model lever and re-runs the full game set. It shows that the central finding is stable within the demonstrative assumptions:

- capitation reweighting improves outcomes modestly;
- a Primary Care Benefits Schedule improves access and hospital-pressure logic;
- the full upstream access architecture performs best;
- loose benefits with weak controls increase gaming and equity risk.

The weak-control scenario is deliberately included because the policy should not be framed as unrestricted activity funding.

## Policy-relevant interpretation

The emerging policy line is:

> **Demand-driven within rules; not demand-driven without rules.**

That means public subsidy should be available for eligible primary care, urgent care and ambulance/prehospital contact types, but only where:

- the provider is accredited;
- the activity is within scope;
- clinical governance is clear;
- co-payment settings are transparent and equity-protected;
- claims are auditable;
- data can link upstream contacts to downstream hospital outcomes;
- gaming, fragmentation and low-value volume are monitored.

## What needs to be empirically shown

Before making predictive claims, the model needs to show:

1. whether weak marginal payment actually constrains supply;
2. whether capitation reweighting alone leaves total supply constrained;
3. whether PHO intermediation creates measurable transaction cost or market-entry barriers;
4. whether ACC activity funding stabilises primary care viability;
5. whether ambulance alternatives reduce ED pressure safely;
6. whether broader provider eligibility expands supply without compromising safety;
7. whether co-payment settings moderate use without worsening delayed care;
8. whether primary care and ambulance KPIs change managerial and budget behaviour;
9. whether upstream investment reduces ED and hospital pressure over time;
10. whether equity protections are strong enough for Māori, Pacific, rural and high-need groups.

## Highest-priority data requests

The next data requests should target:

- the current and proposed capitation formula and rate tables;
- PHO pass-through, financial-flow and contract information;
- ACC primary care claim volumes and payment mix;
- ambulance disposition, non-conveyance and ED handover data;
- primary care access and appointment data;
- practice co-payment and enrolment/open-book data;
- ED and potentially preventable hospitalisation data linked by region and population need.

## Suggested decision standard

A RACMA or sector policy paper should not claim that the proposed architecture is proven. It can say:

> **The current primary care reform debate appears focused on distribution within the existing model. The larger architecture question is whether constrained lower-cost access shifts growth into hospitals. This hypothesis is now mapped, testable and ready for empirical calibration.**

## Recommended next action

Commission or partner on a short empirical validation phase:

1. rapid evidence map;
2. OIA/data request package;
3. stakeholder validation workshops;
4. calibrated model prototype;
5. equity impact review;
6. policy options paper.
