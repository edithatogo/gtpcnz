# Policy brief 1: A Primary Care Benefits Schedule for Aotearoa New Zealand

## Problem

New Zealand's current primary care reform pathway is focused on reweighting capitation. Reweighting may improve distribution, but it may not solve the marginal supply problem created when additional clinically necessary activity is weakly funded or administratively constrained.

## Proposal

Create a nationally administered **Primary Care Benefits Schedule** for defined contact types.

This would be a demand-driven public subsidy, not a fixed envelope. It would be constrained at the transaction level by:

- eligible contact type;
- eligible provider acting within scope;
- clinical governance standards;
- patient eligibility and equity settings;
- co-payment rules;
- data reporting;
- audit and anti-gaming safeguards.

## What remains of capitation

Capitation remains for:

- enrolled-population responsibility;
- continuity;
- practice/team baseline viability;
- proactive care;
- population health;
- equity-adjusted funding.

It should not be the dominant mechanism for timely access.

## Eligible contact-type examples

- urgent same-day/next-day primary care;
- complex consultation;
- chronic disease review;
- medicines optimisation;
- stable-repeat prescribing review;
- pharmacist-led minor condition care;
- physiotherapist-led musculoskeletal assessment;
- nurse practitioner-led acute and chronic care;
- mental health brief intervention;
- wound care;
- rural in-person assessment;
- paramedic treat-and-refer;
- ambulance hear-and-treat pathway.

## Provider principle

Funding should follow clinically appropriate activity, not professional guild boundaries. Scope of practice, competence, credentialing and governance should determine which providers can deliver each contact type.

## Co-payment principle

Co-payments can provide a demand signal, but must not become the equity failure mechanism. Options include:

- zero or low co-payments for children and high-needs groups;
- capped co-payments for essential contacts;
- higher public subsidy for deprivation, Māori and Pacific peoples, rurality and multimorbidity;
- monitoring of unmet need by price barrier.

## Key modelling question

Does a demand-driven benefits schedule expand lower-cost primary and prehospital care enough to reduce avoidable ED and hospital growth, while remaining fiscally and equitably sustainable?
