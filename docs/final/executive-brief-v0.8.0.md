# Executive brief v0.8.0

## Primary care funding architecture in Aotearoa New Zealand

**Status:** Final demonstrative synthesis, not empirical proof.  
**Core thesis:** New Zealand may be managing primary care and ambulance funding so tightly that unmet need is channelled into hospitals, where demand becomes more visible, urgent and fundable.

## Recommended architecture

A **National Primary Care Benefits Schedule** layered onto capitation:

- capitation for continuity, enrolment, baseline viability and population accountability;
- demand-driven benefits for eligible primary, urgent, chronic, procedural and ambulance/prehospital contact types;
- provider eligibility based on scope, credentialing and clinical governance, not professional category alone;
- direct or optional claiming through a national rules-based platform;
- PHO/locality functions preserved where useful, but not necessarily as mandatory payment gateways;
- co-payment protections for equity;
- ambulance alternatives funded and measured as access infrastructure;
- top-tier KPIs for primary care and ambulance alongside hospital targets.

## Model finding

The v0.8.0 hybrid model compares five scenarios.

| Scenario | Hybrid viability | Interpretation |
|---|---:|---|
| S0 Status quo tight control | 15.85 | Status quo equilibrium: upstream sectors are tightly managed while hospital pressure remains visible and fundable. |
| S1 Capitation reweighting only | 27.38 | Improves allocation inside capitation but leaves the marginal supply and hospital-rescue games largely intact. |
| S2 Primary Care Benefits Schedule | 50.00 | Materially improves supply and hospital-pressure logic, but still needs stronger ambulance, equity, data and governance architecture. |
| S3 Full upstream access architecture | 65.23 | Best-performing demonstrative architecture: demand-driven where eligible, but governed by scope, safety, data, KPIs and equity protections. |
| S4 Loose benefits, weak controls | 32.57 | Shows why benefits cannot be loose: access rises, but fiscal/gaming/equity risks weaken viability. |


## Bottom line

The model supports a middle path:

> **Demand-driven within rules; not demand-driven without rules.**

The next step is empirical validation through OIA/data access, stakeholder validation, equity/Te Tiriti review and calibrated simulation.
