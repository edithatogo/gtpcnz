## Purpose

Develop a rigorous, publishable and policy-relevant body of work on primary care funding architecture in Australia and Aotearoa New Zealand.

The work will combine:

- microeconomic theory;
- transaction-cost economics;
- principal-agent analysis;
- game-theoretic mapping and modelling;
- system dynamics;
- agent-based simulation;
- evidence review;
- policy briefs;
- public translation through Substack;
- academic publication, initially targeting NZMJ.

## Core users

1. Public Substack readers seeking a plain-English explanation of the funding architecture problem.
2. NZ and Australian primary care, urgent care, ambulance, rural and equity stakeholders.
3. Treasury, Ministry of Health, Health NZ, ACC and Australian Commonwealth/state health policy officials.
4. General practice, rural health, ambulance and community-health leaders.
5. Māori, Pacific, rural, disability and consumer reviewers.
6. NZMJ readers: clinicians, health service managers, policymakers and researchers.

## Primary problem

Primary care reform is often framed as a funding quantum issue. This project reframes it as a funding architecture, incentive and system-equilibrium issue.

The key concern is that tight control of lower-cost upstream services may suppress marginal supply, increase market-entry barriers and shift unmet need into hospitals, where demand is more visible, urgent and expensive.

## Working hypothesis

Aotearoa New Zealand's dominant capitation/PHO-mediated architecture may be missing the whole-system problem by focusing on allocation within primary care rather than the effect of upstream constraints on hospital growth.

Australia has the converse problem: MBS fee-for-service supports marginal supply, but Commonwealth-state split incentives can weaken whole-system investment in hospital avoidance.

## Proposed solution space

- Blended funding, but not merely more capitation.
- A demand-driven, nationally administered Primary Care Benefits Schedule for defined contact types.
- Direct claims through a rules-based payment platform.
- Activity generated by any accredited provider within scope and clinical governance.
- Co-payment as a demand signal, with equity protections.
- Primary care and ambulance top-tier KPIs alongside hospital targets.
- Simulation modelling to test equilibrium effects and unintended consequences.
