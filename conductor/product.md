## Purpose

Develop a rigorous, publishable and policy-relevant body of work on primary care funding architecture in Australia and Aotearoa New Zealand.

The work will combine:

- microeconomic theory;
- transaction-cost economics;
- principal-agent analysis;
- game-theoretic mapping and modelling;
- system dynamics;
- agent-based simulation;
- evidence review;
- policy briefs;
- public translation through Substack;
- academic publication, initially targeting NZMJ.

## Core users

1. Public Substack readers seeking a plain-English explanation of the funding architecture problem.
2. NZ and Australian primary care, urgent care, ambulance, rural and equity stakeholders.
3. Treasury, Ministry of Health, Health NZ, ACC and Australian Commonwealth/state health policy officials.
4. General practice, rural health, ambulance and community-health leaders.
5. Māori, Pacific, rural, disability and consumer reviewers.
6. NZMJ readers: clinicians, health service managers, policymakers and researchers.

## Primary problem

Primary care reform is often framed as a funding quantum issue. This project reframes it as a funding architecture, incentive and system-equilibrium issue.

The key concern is that tight control of lower-cost upstream services may suppress marginal supply, increase market-entry barriers and shift unmet need into hospitals, where demand is more visible, urgent and expensive.

## Working hypothesis

Aotearoa New Zealand's current reform pathway is real and should be treated as the comparator. The open question is whether capitation reweighting, access targets, data infrastructure, digital care, urgent-care investment and PHO accountability change the upstream supply game enough, or whether unmet need still flows into hospitals because marginal primary medical activity remains weakly funded.

Australia has the converse problem: MBS fee-for-service supports marginal supply, but Commonwealth-state split incentives can weaken whole-system investment in hospital avoidance.

## Proposed solution space

- Capitation for continuity, enrolment, baseline viability and population responsibility.
- An uncapped-at-envelope, scheduled, rules-based, audited fee-for-service stream for eligible primary medical activity.
- Place-based accountability and equity obligations to prevent cherry-picking.
- Direct claims or transparent payment flows where appropriate, without losing locality/population functions.
- Activity generated by any accredited provider within scope, competence and clinical governance.
- Co-payment protections so price is not the mechanism of rationing for high-need groups.
- Primary care, urgent care and ambulance top-tier KPIs alongside hospital targets.
- Simulation modelling used to prioritise validation, not to claim calibrated predictions.

## v1.7.2 risk-controlled stance
The current reform pathway is treated as the main comparator. The project avoids predictive claims and uses the phrase 'uncapped at the activity-envelope level, controlled at item/scope/audit/data/accountability level'.
